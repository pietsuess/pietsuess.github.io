How Breathing Patterns Affect Your Body

By Selena La Brooy • September 15, 2024

Take a moment right now and notice your breathing. Where does the breath move in your body? Does your chest rise and fall? Do your ribs expand to the sides? Does your belly soften and fill? Most of us rarely think about how we breathe, yet our breathing patterns have a profound effect on the way our bodies feel and function every single day.

When we are stressed, anxious, or simply sitting hunched over a screen for hours, our breathing tends to become shallow and confined to the upper chest. Over time, this restricted pattern becomes the norm. The muscles between the ribs, known as the intercostals, lose their flexibility. The diaphragm, that dome-shaped muscle at the base of the ribcage, becomes tight and limited in its range of motion. The accessory breathing muscles in the neck and shoulders pick up the slack, leading to chronic tension in areas that were never designed to be the primary drivers of respiration.

This is where things get interesting from a structural perspective. The fascia surrounding the ribcage, diaphragm, and thoracic spine adapts to these restricted patterns. Connective tissue is incredibly responsive to the demands placed on it. If the ribs rarely expand fully, the fascia around them will gradually shorten and thicken, reinforcing the very pattern that caused the restriction in the first place. It becomes a self-perpetuating cycle.

In my Rolfing practice, I see this pattern frequently. Clients come in with neck pain, shoulder tension, or low back discomfort, and when we begin to explore what is happening structurally, restricted breathing is often a significant contributing factor. The thorax is the centre of the body. When it is locked down, everything above and below has to compensate.

Rolfing addresses this by working directly with the fascial layers around the ribcage and diaphragm. Through careful, sustained pressure, we can encourage the connective tissue to release and lengthen. As the ribs regain their ability to move freely, the diaphragm can descend more fully with each inhalation. Clients often describe the feeling as though they are breathing into parts of their body they had forgotten existed.

The benefits extend well beyond the ribcage. When breathing deepens, the nervous system responds. The parasympathetic branch, responsible for rest and recovery, becomes more active. Stress hormones decrease. Chronic pain patterns that were being amplified by a heightened stress response begin to settle. It is remarkable how much can shift when the body simply has more room to breathe.

There are also things you can explore on your own. Try this simple practice: sit comfortably and place your hands on the sides of your lower ribs. As you inhale, see if you can direct the breath sideways into your hands. You are not trying to force anything. Just invite the ribs to expand laterally. Do this for five or six breaths, a few times throughout the day. You may notice that your shoulders begin to drop, your neck softens, and there is a general sense of ease that was not there before.

Another helpful practice is to lie on your back with your knees bent and simply observe your breathing for a few minutes. Notice what moves and what stays still. Over time, the act of paying attention to the breath can begin to change the pattern on its own. The body is remarkably good at self-correcting when given the right input.

If you find that your breathing feels consistently shallow or effortful, it may be worth exploring what structural factors are contributing. Sometimes all the body needs is a little help releasing the fascial restrictions that have accumulated over years of habitual patterning. That is exactly what Rolfing is designed to do.
