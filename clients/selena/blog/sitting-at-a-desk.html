What Sitting All Day Does to Your Body

By Selena La Brooy • June 8, 2024

If you work at a desk, you already know the feeling. You start the day sitting up reasonably well, and by mid-afternoon your chin has drifted forward, your shoulders have rounded in, and your lower back aches in a way that has become so familiar you barely notice it. You stand up and everything feels stiff. You stretch, shake it off, and sit back down. The cycle repeats.

What many people do not realize is that prolonged sitting creates very specific structural changes in the body over time. These are not just temporary stiffness. They are patterns that become embedded in the connective tissue, gradually reshaping how the body holds itself even when you are standing and moving.

Let us look at what actually happens. When you sit for extended periods, the hip flexors at the front of the pelvis are held in a shortened position for hours at a time. The fascia in this area adapts by tightening and thickening, pulling the pelvis into an anterior tilt. This places extra load on the lower back, which now has to work harder to keep you upright.

Meanwhile, the head drifts forward to get closer to the screen. For every inch the head moves forward of its ideal position over the spine, the muscles at the back of the neck have to work significantly harder to hold it up. The fascia along the back of the neck and into the upper shoulders thickens and shortens in response. This is one of the most common sources of headaches and neck pain I see in my practice.

The shoulders round forward as the arms reach toward a keyboard or mouse. The pectoral muscles and the fascia across the front of the chest shorten. The upper back muscles become overstretched and weakened. Over months and years, this pattern becomes structural. The body begins to hold this shape not because the muscles are actively contracting, but because the fascial web has reorganized around the pattern.

Here is what I find encouraging. These patterns are not permanent. The fascia is a living, responsive tissue. It shortened and thickened in response to the demands of sitting, and it can be lengthened and reorganized in response to different input. That is precisely what Rolfing does.

In a Rolfing session, we work systematically through the layers of fascia that have adapted to the sitting pattern. We release the shortened hip flexors, open up the front of the chest, restore mobility to the thoracic spine, and bring the head back over the body’s centre of gravity. Clients often describe the sensation of standing after a session as feeling taller, lighter, and more open. They are not imagining it. The structural relationships have genuinely changed.

Of course, Rolfing works best when combined with conscious movement throughout the day. Here are a few simple practices you can start with. Every 30 to 45 minutes, stand up and take a short walk, even if it is just to the kitchen and back. While standing, place your hands on your lower back and gently arch backward a few times to counter the flexed position of sitting. Roll your shoulders back and let your arms hang naturally at your sides for a moment.

Another helpful practice is to sit on the front edge of your chair rather than leaning against the backrest. This engages your core muscles and encourages a more upright posture. It may feel tiring at first, so alternate between this and your usual seated position.

The modern world asks most of us to sit far more than our bodies were designed for. We cannot always change our work circumstances, but we can change how our bodies respond to them. If sitting has left you with chronic tightness, pain, or a posture that does not feel like it belongs to you, Rolfing can help you find your way back to a body that feels balanced and at ease.
