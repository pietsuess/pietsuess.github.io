Rolfing for Athletes and Active Bodies

By Selena La Brooy • March 22, 2025

If you are someone who moves your body regularly, whether you run, hike, climb, practice yoga, or play a sport, you probably already have a good sense of what your body can do. You may also have a growing awareness of where it gets stuck. That persistent tightness in one hip, the shoulder that never quite feels free, the knee that complains on steep descents. These are the kinds of patterns that bring active people to Rolfing.

Every form of movement creates specific demands on the body. Running, for example, is a beautifully repetitive activity. The same muscles fire in the same sequence, stride after stride, kilometre after kilometre. Over time, the fascia adapts to support this particular movement pattern. The tissues that are used most become denser and more defined. But this specialization comes at a cost. Areas that are not being asked to participate become less mobile, and the body gradually loses some of its overall adaptability.

I see this frequently with trail runners and hikers here on Salt Spring Island. The terrain is hilly and varied, which is wonderful for building strength, but the repetitive nature of climbing and descending creates predictable patterns. The calves and hip flexors tighten. The IT band becomes dense and restricted. The ankles lose some of their lateral mobility. None of these changes are injuries in themselves, but they set the stage for injury by reducing the body’s ability to adapt to unexpected demands.

Climbers present a different but equally recognizable pattern. The forearms, shoulders, and lats become extraordinarily strong and developed, while the front of the body may become relatively shortened. The fascial balance between the front and back of the body shifts, and over time this can affect everything from breathing capacity to low back comfort.

Yoga practitioners sometimes come to Rolfing with a different kind of imbalance. They may have excellent flexibility in certain ranges of motion, but the connective tissue has become so elastic in some areas that stability is compromised. Rolfing can help by differentiating the layers of fascia and bringing more organization to the relationships between muscles, so that flexibility is supported by structural integrity.

What Rolfing offers athletes is not just pain relief or recovery, though those are valuable benefits. The deeper gift is improved structural efficiency. When the fascial web is well organized, the body moves with less internal friction. Range of motion increases not because muscles have been stretched, but because the connective tissue is allowing movement to flow through the body more freely. Many athletes report that after a series of Rolfing sessions, their movements feel smoother and more effortless. They can access power they did not know was available.

Recovery is another area where Rolfing makes a real difference. When fascia is restricted, circulation to the surrounding tissues is reduced. Metabolic waste from exercise does not clear as efficiently, and fresh nutrients are slower to arrive. By releasing fascial adhesions and restoring healthy tissue hydration, Rolfing supports the body’s natural recovery processes. Many of my athletic clients schedule sessions during periods of heavy training or after particularly demanding events.

Injury prevention is perhaps the most significant long-term benefit. Most sports injuries do not happen randomly. They happen in areas where the body has been compensating for a structural imbalance. A runner with restricted ankle mobility will eventually place excessive strain on the knee or hip. A climber with a tight thorax will overwork the shoulder. By addressing these imbalances before they become injuries, Rolfing helps active people stay in the activities they love.

If you are active and curious about what Rolfing could do for your performance and longevity in your sport, I would love to have that conversation. Every body tells a unique story, and understanding the patterns your activities have created is the first step toward moving with greater freedom and resilience.
