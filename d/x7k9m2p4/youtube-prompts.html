35 ChatGPT Prompts for Titles, Scripts, Thumbnails & Channel Growth — Copy, paste, customize the [BRACKETED] parts.

Titles & Thumbnails (1–9)

1Viral Title Generator
Generate 20 YouTube title options for a video about [TOPIC]. Mix these proven formats: how-to, listicle, challenge, story-driven, controversial take, and curiosity gap. Each title must be under 60 characters. After each title, rate it 1-10 for click-through potential and briefly explain why. My niche is [NICHE]. My target audience is [AUDIENCE]. Prioritize titles that would work for a channel with [SUBSCRIBER COUNT] subscribers. At the end, pick your top 3 and explain what makes them stand out from the rest.
Tip: Run this prompt twice with slightly different topic angles. The second batch is often more creative because ChatGPT moves past the obvious options.
2Thumbnail Concept Designer
Design 5 thumbnail concepts for my YouTube video titled "[VIDEO TITLE]". For each concept, describe: (1) the image composition and layout, (2) the text overlay (3-4 words maximum, large and readable), (3) the facial expression or pose if a person is featured, (4) the color scheme and dominant colors, (5) the primary emotion it should trigger in the viewer. Reference what currently works in the [NICHE] space on YouTube. All thumbnails must be readable at mobile phone size (small screen). Rank the 5 concepts from safest to most bold, and note which one you'd pick for a channel trying to grow.
Tip: Feed the winning concept into an image generator like Midjourney or DALL-E for a mockup before sending it to a designer.
3Title A/B Test Variants
I'm publishing a YouTube video about [TOPIC]. Give me 3 pairs of A/B test titles. For each pair, write: (A) a safe, clear, search-friendly option, and (B) a risky, curiosity-driven, emotionally provocative option. For every title, explain the psychology behind it — what cognitive bias or emotional trigger it leverages. Then advise which type (safe vs. risky) tends to perform better for a [CHANNEL SIZE: small/mid/large] channel and why. Finally, suggest one hybrid title per pair that combines the best of both approaches.
Tip: Use TubeBuddy or VidIQ to actually run A/B tests on your top pair. Data beats gut feeling every time.
4Clickbait vs. Value Title Line
Write 10 YouTube titles for a video about [TOPIC] that ride the line between clickbait and genuine value. Every title must: create strong curiosity, accurately represent the video content, and avoid being misleading. After the 10 titles, show me 3 examples of titles that cross the line into dishonest clickbait for the same topic, and explain exactly why they're problematic. Then show 3 examples that are too boring and undersell the content. Teach me to recognize the difference so I can write my own in the future.
Tip: The golden rule — if a viewer clicks and feels tricked within 30 seconds, the title went too far. If they don't click at all, it didn't go far enough.
5Search-Optimized Title
Write an SEO-optimized YouTube title for a video about [TOPIC]. Target keywords: [LIST OF KEYWORDS]. The title must: include the primary keyword within the first 4 words, be under 60 characters total, and be compelling enough to earn a click even without seeing the thumbnail. Also write the first 2 lines of the video description (the part visible above the fold before "Show more") — these lines should include the primary keyword naturally and give viewers a reason to watch. Finally, suggest 3 alternative titles optimized for different long-tail keyword variations of the same topic.
Tip: Check YouTube's search autocomplete for your primary keyword. If your exact phrase appears in suggestions, you have confirmed search demand.
6Series Title Framework
I'm creating a [NUMBER]-part YouTube series about [TOPIC]. Create a naming convention and title for every episode that satisfies three requirements: (1) each video works individually — someone finding it in search understands what it's about without context, (2) the titles clearly signal they belong to a connected series, (3) each title builds momentum and makes the viewer want to watch the next part. Include all episode titles. Also suggest: the series name/brand, the ideal playlist title (keyword-rich), and whether I should number them (e.g., "Part 3") or use sequential descriptors. Explain the tradeoffs of each approach.
Tip: Avoid "Part 1, Part 2" in titles when possible — it signals commitment and scares off new viewers. Use descriptive subtitles instead.
7Reaction/Response Title
I'm making a reaction or response video to [CONTENT/CREATOR/TREND]. Write 10 title options that: reference the original content without being parasitic or disrespectful, clearly convey my unique angle which is [MY PERSPECTIVE], and go beyond generic "I react to X" framing. Include both collaborative-tone titles (I appreciate/agree/build on) and contrarian-tone titles (I disagree/challenge/correct). For each title, note whether it's more likely to attract the original creator's audience, my existing audience, or new search traffic. Flag any that risk starting drama unintentionally.
Tip: Collaborative titles build bridges with other creators. Contrarian titles get more clicks short-term but can burn relationships. Choose strategically.
8Trending Topic Title
There's a trending topic right now in [NICHE]: [DESCRIBE THE TREND]. Write 5 YouTube titles designed to ride this trend within the next 24 hours. Each title must: connect the trend directly to my expertise and angle [MY ANGLE], feel timely without being opportunistic or exploitative, and still work as a standalone video even after the trend dies down. For each, estimate how long the trend window is (hours/days/weeks) and which title I could realistically film and upload in under 2 hours. Prioritize speed-to-publish for the top recommendation.
Tip: For trending content, "good enough and fast" always beats "perfect and late." Ship within 4 hours of the trend peaking.
9Thumbnail Text Psychology
For my YouTube video titled "[TITLE]", write 10 short text overlay options for the thumbnail. Each must be 2-4 words maximum and designed to trigger a specific emotion: (1) shock, (2) curiosity, (3) FOMO, (4) humor, (5) controversy, (6) aspiration, (7) fear, (8) relief, (9) anger, (10) excitement. For each, explain why that emotion works on thumbnails and which emotion historically performs best in the [NICHE] space. Also specify the ideal font style (bold sans-serif, handwritten, etc.) and color for each overlay. End with your #1 recommendation and why.
Tip: Thumbnail text and title should complement each other, not repeat each other. If your title says "I quit my job," the thumbnail text should say something like "no regrets" — not "I quit."

Scripting & Content (10–21)

10Full Video Script
Write a complete script for a [LENGTH]-minute YouTube video about [TOPIC]. Use this structure: (1) Hook — first 8 seconds, use a pattern interrupt that stops the scroll (question, bold claim, or visual surprise). (2) Intro — establish credibility and clearly state what the viewer will learn or experience. (3) Main body — 3-5 key points, each with a smooth transition. Vary the pacing so energy doesn't flatline. (4) CTA placement — place subscribe and like prompts at natural emotional peaks, never forced or begging. (5) Close — strong ending that callbacks to the hook, creating a satisfying loop. Target audience: [AUDIENCE]. Tone: [educational/entertaining/inspirational]. Include stage directions in brackets for on-screen graphics, B-roll, or emphasis changes.
Tip: Read the script out loud and time it. A natural speaking pace is roughly 150 words per minute. Cut anything that feels like filler when spoken aloud.
11Hook Library
Write 15 different hooks (the opening 10-15 seconds) for a YouTube video about [TOPIC]. Mix these hook types: (1) provocative question, (2) bold/controversial claim, (3) story opening ("Last Tuesday, I..."), (4) surprising statistic, (5) live demonstration, (6) controversy or hot take, (7) "Don't do this" warning, (8) mystery or intrigue, (9) time pressure or urgency, (10) social proof. Each hook must stop the scroll within the first 3 seconds and prevent click-away within 5 seconds. After all 15, rank your top 5 and explain why they'd have the highest retention in the first 30 seconds. Note which hooks work best for [NICHE] specifically.
Tip: Film your top 3 hooks and post them as YouTube Shorts. Whichever gets the best retention, use that as your long-form video opener.
12YouTube Shorts Script
Write 5 YouTube Shorts scripts (each under 60 seconds when spoken) derived from this longer video topic: [TOPIC/MAIN VIDEO IDEA]. Each Short must: hook the viewer in the first 2 seconds with a bold statement or visual, deliver exactly one complete and valuable idea (no cliffhangers that require the long-form video), and end with a reason to follow or subscribe that feels natural, not forced. For each script, include: the spoken dialogue with timing notes, suggested text overlays and when they appear on screen, and a note on whether it works best as talking head, screen recording, or B-roll with voiceover. Indicate which Short is most likely to go viral and why.
Tip: The best-performing Shorts are ones that viewers watch 2-3 times because the loop is seamless. Design your ending to flow naturally back into the beginning.
13Tutorial Script Structure
Write a tutorial script for "How to [TASK]" aimed at a [BEGINNER/INTERMEDIATE/ADVANCED] audience. Structure: (1) Show the end result first — what we're building or achieving, so viewers know the payoff. (2) Prerequisites — what they need before starting (software, accounts, knowledge). Keep this under 30 seconds. (3) Step-by-step walkthrough with suggested timestamp markers for each major step. (4) At each step, include the most common mistake people make and how to avoid it. (5) Final result showcase and suggested next steps or related tutorials. Keep it concise and ruthlessly cut any tangent. Tutorial viewers have zero patience for rambling — if a sentence doesn't move them closer to the result, delete it.
Tip: Add chapters (timestamps) in your description matching each step. Viewers who can skip ahead have higher retention than those who click away.
14Talking Head Script with B-Roll Notes
Write a talking-head script for a YouTube video about [TOPIC] with detailed B-roll suggestions throughout. Format each section in two layers: [TALKING HEAD] with the exact dialogue and delivery notes (pace, emphasis, tone shifts), then [B-ROLL: description of supporting footage, graphics, or screen recordings to show during this section]. Include [ON-SCREEN TEXT: key phrases or data points that should appear as text overlays]. Target length: [MINUTES] minutes. Ensure the B-roll suggestions are practical — things I can actually film or source from stock footage. Flag any sections that are "talking head heavy" and suggest how to break them up visually.
Tip: The general rule is no more than 15 seconds of unbroken talking head before cutting to B-roll. Viewers process information better with visual variety.
15Story-Based Video Script
Write a story-driven YouTube video script about [TOPIC/EXPERIENCE]. Use this narrative framework: (1) Ordinary world — set the scene, who I was before. (2) Inciting incident — the event that changed everything. (3) Rising action — the struggle, the attempts, the setbacks. Make this specific with concrete details, not abstract. (4) Crisis/turning point — the lowest moment or the breakthrough. (5) Resolution — what happened and how things changed. (6) Lesson — the takeaway for the viewer, positioned as something they can use in their own life. Include emotional pacing notes (where to slow down, where to build intensity, where to pause). The script should feel personal and genuine, not like a motivational speech. Include [MUSIC CUE] suggestions for key emotional moments.
Tip: Specific beats resonate more than grand statements. "I stared at $47 in my bank account" hits harder than "I was broke."
16Listicle Video Script
Write a script for a YouTube video titled "[NUMBER] [THINGS] for [AUDIENCE/PURPOSE]". For each item on the list: (1) open with a teaser — why this item earned its spot, (2) explain what it is and why it matters, (3) give a concrete example or proof that it works, (4) transition smoothly to the next item (no "okay, number 4 is..."). Structure the list strategically: open with the most surprising or counterintuitive item to hook viewers past the first minute, bury the weakest items in the middle, and close with the most valuable item to reward viewers who stay. Include a brief intro that promises value and a close that summarizes the top 3.
Tip: Tease a later item early in the video ("Wait until you see number 7 — it changed everything for me"). This curiosity loop boosts watch time dramatically.
17Comparison Video Script
Write a YouTube script comparing [THING A] vs [THING B] for [USE CASE]. Structure: (1) Intro — why this comparison matters right now and who should care. (2) Establish 5 fair comparison criteria — categories that actually matter to the viewer's decision (not just specs for the sake of specs). (3) Head-to-head breakdown for each category with a clear winner per round. Be specific with examples, not vague. (4) Verdict — a definitive recommendation for different user types (e.g., "If you're [X], pick A. If you're [Y], pick B"). Avoid the cop-out "it depends" conclusion. (5) My personal recommendation with honest reasoning, including what I actually use and why. Include a visual scoreboard concept for on-screen graphics.
Tip: Comparison videos are search magnets. People type "X vs Y" constantly. Optimize your title for that exact search query.
18Video Essay Script
Write a video essay script for YouTube about [TOPIC/THESIS]. My thesis is: [YOUR ARGUMENT]. Structure: (1) Cold open — a compelling real-world example, anecdote, or scene that immediately illustrates why this topic matters. Don't state the thesis yet. (2) State the thesis clearly after the viewer is hooked. (3) Present 3 supporting arguments, each backed by evidence (studies, examples, data, expert opinions). (4) Steelman the best counterargument, then dismantle it fairly. (5) Conclusion that reframes the opening example in light of everything we've discussed, creating a satisfying full-circle moment. Tone: thoughtful and nuanced, never preachy or condescending. Target length: [MINUTES] minutes. Include [VISUAL: ] notes for supporting footage, graphics, or archival material throughout.
Tip: The best video essays make the viewer feel smart, not lectured. Present evidence and let the conclusion feel like their own realization.
19Podcast/Interview Questions
Generate 20 interview questions for [GUEST NAME/DESCRIPTION] appearing on my YouTube channel. The conversation topic is [TOPIC]. Organize the questions in this flow: (1) Backstory questions — their origin, how they got started, an early defining moment. (2) Deep expertise questions — the insights only they can share from their experience. (3) Controversial or spicy questions — things that spark debate and create clip-worthy moments. (4) Practical advice — actionable takeaways viewers can use immediately. (5) Rapid-fire round — 5 quick questions with unexpected/fun prompts. (6) One question that nobody ever asks them — designed to surprise and get a genuine, unrehearsed answer. Order all questions to build rapport before going deep. Note which questions are most likely to produce viral clips for Shorts.
Tip: Send your guest 5-6 questions in advance so they feel prepared, but keep your best 3-4 as surprises. Authentic reactions make the best content.
20Community Post Ideas
Write 15 YouTube Community tab posts for my channel about [NICHE]. Mix these formats: (1) polls that spark debate (2-4 options), (2) image posts with a provocative question or hot take, (3) text-only updates that feel personal and behind-the-scenes, (4) "this or that" questions related to my niche, (5) upcoming video teasers that build anticipation without spoiling, (6) milestone celebrations that feel grateful not braggy, (7) ask-me-anything style questions that source future video ideas. Each post should prioritize driving comments (comments > likes > shares for algorithm). Include a suggested posting schedule — how many per week and what time of day works best for engagement on the Community tab.
Tip: Community posts with polls get 3-5x more engagement than text-only posts. YouTube also shows polls to non-subscribers, giving you free reach.
21Content Repurposing Plan
I have a [LENGTH]-minute YouTube video about [TOPIC]. Turn this single piece of content into a full repurposing plan: (1) 3 YouTube Shorts scripts — each pulling a different standalone insight from the video. (2) 5 tweet or Twitter/X thread ideas that tease key points and link back. (3) 2 Instagram carousel concepts with slide-by-slide outlines (what goes on each of 8-10 slides). (4) 1 blog post outline that expands on the video for SEO. (5) 1 email newsletter edition for my subscriber list. Each repurposed piece must stand completely on its own while also driving traffic back to the original YouTube video. Include the CTA and link placement strategy for each platform.
Tip: Repurpose within 48 hours of publishing the original video. The algorithm favors content that drives external traffic during the initial push.

SEO & Growth (22–30)

22Video Description Template
Write a fully SEO-optimized YouTube video description for my video titled "[VIDEO TITLE]". Structure it as follows: (1) First 2 lines (above the fold, visible before "Show more") — a compelling hook with the primary keyword that makes viewers want to watch. (2) A paragraph summarizing the video content with keywords [LIST OF KEYWORDS] integrated naturally, not stuffed. (3) Timestamps/chapters for each major section. (4) Relevant links section (social media, referenced resources, related videos). (5) 3-5 relevant hashtags. (6) An "About This Channel" boilerplate paragraph for [CHANNEL NAME] in the [NICHE] space. Total description length: 500-1000 words. The description should help YouTube understand the video's topic while also convincing humans to watch.
Tip: The first 150 characters of your description show in search results. Treat those characters like ad copy — they need to sell the click.
23Tag & Hashtag Strategy
Generate a complete tag strategy for my YouTube video about [TOPIC]. Primary keyword: [MAIN KEYWORD]. Create tags in these categories: (1) Exact match — the primary keyword as-is. (2) Broad match — related topics and synonyms. (3) Long-tail variations — specific phrases people search. (4) Competitor/related channel names (where relevant and ethical). (5) Trending related terms that connect to this topic right now. Also suggest 3-5 hashtags to place in the title or description. Explain the overall tag strategy: how many tags to use, which to prioritize, and how tagging differs for [SMALL/MEDIUM/LARGE] channels. Note which tags target browse traffic vs. search traffic vs. suggested videos.
Tip: Tags matter less than they used to, but they still help YouTube understand your content in the first 48 hours. Focus your energy on title and thumbnail first.
24Content Calendar (Monthly)
Create a detailed 4-week content calendar for my YouTube channel about [NICHE]. My posting schedule is [X VIDEOS PER WEEK]. Use this content mix: 40% search/evergreen (videos that rank in search for years), 30% trending/timely (riding current topics in the niche), 20% series/recurring (building habitual viewership), 10% experimental (new formats, topics, or styles). For each video slot, provide: a working title, the content type (search/trending/series/experimental), the target primary keyword, estimated search volume (low/medium/high), and a one-line content angle. Also note which videos to promote as Shorts and which pair well for end-screen suggestions. Include the best day and time to publish each type.
Tip: Batch-plan monthly but stay flexible weekly. If a major trend hits your niche, swap out a search/evergreen video for a trending piece.
25Niche Research & Positioning
I want to start (or reposition) my YouTube channel about [BROAD TOPIC]. Help me find my niche within the niche. Analyze: (1) What subtopics are oversaturated with big creators who are hard to compete with? (2) What subtopics are underserved — real search demand but few quality videos? (3) Where does my unique background [MY BACKGROUND/SKILLS/EXPERIENCE] give me a genuine advantage? Suggest 3 specific positioning statements in the format: "I help [AUDIENCE] achieve [RESULT] through [MY UNIQUE METHOD]." For each positioning, generate 10 video ideas that only I could credibly make. Explain which position has the best growth trajectory over the next 12 months and why.
Tip: The riches are in the niches. A channel for "beginner watercolor artists over 40" will grow faster than a generic "art tutorial" channel because every video resonates deeply with a specific viewer.
26Competitor Analysis
Analyze these YouTube channels in my niche: [LIST 3-5 CHANNEL NAMES]. For each channel, evaluate: (1) What they do exceptionally well (format, editing, personality, consistency). (2) Their content gaps — topics they haven't covered or audiences they're neglecting. (3) Their upload frequency and patterns (how often, what days, any seasonal trends). (4) Their thumbnail and title style — common patterns and what makes them click-worthy. (5) Their audience engagement (comment sentiment, community interaction). After individual analysis, synthesize: what opportunities exist that none of these channels are pursuing? What formats or angles could I own? What mistakes are they all making that I can avoid? Give me 5 specific video ideas based on gaps you've identified.
Tip: Don't just watch their popular videos — study their flops. The videos that underperformed reveal topics or formats their audience rejected. Learn from their experiments.
27YouTube Algorithm Cheat Sheet
Based on my current YouTube channel stats — average views per video: [AVERAGE VIEWS], average view duration: [AVG VIEW DURATION], click-through rate: [CTR], subscriber count: [SUBSCRIBER COUNT], niche: [NICHE] — diagnose my channel's performance. What is the YouTube algorithm likely rewarding or penalizing based on these numbers? Identify my weakest metric and explain why it's holding everything else back. Then create a specific 30-day action plan focused on improving the single metric with the highest leverage. Include: what to change in my content, thumbnails, titles, or upload strategy. Give me benchmarks to hit each week and how to measure progress. Be brutally specific, not generic motivation.
Tip: CTR below 4% means your packaging (titles + thumbnails) needs work. Average view duration below 40% means your content or pacing needs work. Fix packaging first — nobody sees great content if they never click.
28Collaboration Pitch
Write a collaboration pitch message to send to [CREATOR NAME/DESCRIPTION/SIZE]. My channel: [YOUR CHANNEL NAME, SIZE, AND NICHE DESCRIPTION]. Explain the mutual benefit clearly — what they gain from working with me, not just what I gain. Propose 3 specific collaboration ideas that: serve both audiences equally, are easy to say yes to (low effort for them), and would create content neither of us could make alone. Keep the message short (under 200 words), genuine (not templated-feeling), and professional without being stiff. Include a subject line that gets opened. Also write a shorter follow-up message for if they don't respond in 7 days.
Tip: Engage genuinely with their content for 2-3 weeks before pitching. Comment thoughtfully on their videos so your name is familiar when the DM arrives.
29Playlist Strategy
Organize my existing YouTube videos into playlists designed to maximize session watch time. My current videos cover these topics: [LIST VIDEO TITLES OR TOPICS]. For each playlist: (1) Create a keyword-rich, benefit-driven playlist name that works in search. (2) Write a playlist description (150+ words with relevant keywords). (3) Specify the video order — which video goes first matters enormously (pick the one with the best hook and broadest appeal). (4) Explain the viewing flow and why this order keeps people watching. Then suggest 3 new playlists I should build toward over the next 3 months, including what videos I'd need to create to fill them. Bonus: which playlist should I feature on my channel page as the default?
Tip: The first video in a playlist gets the most views. Put your best-performing video first, not your oldest. Update playlist order monthly based on analytics.
30Channel Trailer Script
Write a 60-second channel trailer script for [CHANNEL NAME]. Niche: [NICHE]. Upload schedule: [FREQUENCY]. My best/most popular video topics: [TOP 3 TOPICS]. The trailer must: (1) Hook in the first 3 seconds — no "hey guys, welcome to my channel" (ever). Open with a bold statement, a question, or a compelling clip. (2) Clearly communicate the value proposition — what viewers get by subscribing, stated as a benefit to them. (3) Showcase personality and production style so new viewers know what to expect. (4) Include suggestions for where to insert clips from existing videos [CLIP: topic]. (5) End with a clear, confident subscribe CTA. Write it punchy — every second counts. A channel trailer that doesn't earn a subscribe in 60 seconds is too long.
Tip: Your channel trailer should only play for unsubscribed visitors. Set a different "featured video" for returning subscribers (usually your latest upload).

Monetization & Business (31–35)

31Sponsorship Pitch Deck
Write a sponsorship pitch email and one-page overview document for [BRAND NAME] from my YouTube channel [CHANNEL NAME] ([SUBSCRIBER COUNT] subscribers). Include: (1) A compelling opening that connects their brand to my audience (don't lead with "I'd love to work with you"). (2) Audience demographics — age range, interests, purchasing behavior, geography. (3) Engagement stats: [INSERT YOUR AVERAGE VIEWS, CTR, COMMENTS, ETC.]. (4) Why my specific audience matches their target customer, with concrete reasoning. (5) Three integration options: a dedicated video (full sponsorship), an integrated segment (60-90 second mid-roll), and a brief mention (15-30 seconds). (6) Pricing suggestion based on my [SUBSCRIBER COUNT] and typical CPM rates for [NICHE]. Keep the tone professional but human — brands want to work with creators, not corporations.
Tip: A common pricing formula: $20-50 per 1,000 average views for an integrated sponsorship. Dedicated videos command 2-3x more. Always quote higher — brands expect to negotiate down.
32Digital Product Idea Generator
Based on my YouTube content about [NICHE] with [SUBSCRIBER COUNT] subscribers, suggest 5 digital products I could create and sell to my audience. For each product: (1) Product name and tagline. (2) Format — online course, template pack, ebook/guide, community/membership, or toolkit. (3) Price point with reasoning (why this price works for my audience size). (4) The key differentiator — what this product offers that my free YouTube content does not (depth, personalization, structure, access, accountability). (5) Launch strategy using my existing videos as the funnel — which videos drive demand for this product, and how to add CTAs without being pushy. Rank the 5 by ease-of-creation and revenue potential. Which one should I build first?
Tip: Start with the product that solves the #1 question in your YouTube comments. If viewers keep asking the same thing, there's a product in that answer.
33Affiliate Content Strategy
I'm an affiliate for [PRODUCTS/SERVICES]. Create a YouTube content strategy that drives affiliate revenue without being sleazy or eroding audience trust. Include: (1) 5 video formats that naturally and honestly incorporate affiliate products (reviews, comparisons, "what I use" setups, tutorials using the product, "best of" roundups). Write a title and brief concept for each. (2) How to disclose affiliate relationships properly and transparently (FTC compliance). (3) Description and pinned comment link strategy — where to place links for maximum clicks. (4) A "best tools/resources" page concept on my website that serves as a permanent affiliate hub. (5) How to balance affiliate content with non-affiliate content so my channel doesn't feel like one long ad. What ratio works best?
Tip: Only promote products you actually use and believe in. One dishonest recommendation can destroy years of audience trust. If a product isn't great, say so — your honesty builds more affiliate revenue long-term.
34YouTube to Email Funnel
Design a complete funnel: YouTube viewers → email subscribers → product customers for my channel about [NICHE]. Include: (1) Lead magnet concept — a free resource related to my most popular video topics that's compelling enough to trade an email for. Suggest 3 options and pick the best. (2) Where and how to promote the lead magnet on YouTube (description, pinned comment, in-video mention, end screen). (3) A 5-email welcome sequence: Email 1 (deliver the lead magnet + set expectations), Email 2 (quick win / best content), Email 3 (personal story + deeper value), Email 4 (social proof + community), Email 5 (soft pitch for paid product or service). (4) Ongoing nurture strategy — weekly email format and content. Goal: convert [X]% of YouTube subscribers to email list.
Tip: A YouTube subscriber is worth ~$0.01 in ad revenue. An email subscriber is worth $1-5+/month depending on your niche. Building an email list is the highest-leverage move most YouTubers ignore.
35Revenue Diversification Plan
My YouTube channel [CHANNEL NAME] currently makes approximately [AMOUNT/MONTH] from AdSense. Subscriber count: [NUMBER]. Niche: [NICHE]. Create a revenue diversification plan with realistic income projections for each stream: (1) AdSense optimization — how to increase RPM without changing content. (2) Channel memberships — tier structure, perks, and projected uptake. (3) Super Chats/Super Thanks — strategies to increase live and on-demand tipping. (4) Sponsorships — how many per month, pricing, and outreach plan. (5) Affiliate marketing — top 3 programs for my niche. (6) Digital products — what to sell and projected revenue. (7) Merchandise — whether it makes sense at my size, and if so, what to sell. Prioritize all 7 streams by effort-to-income ratio. Include a 6-month roadmap for implementing them in order. Be honest about what's realistic for my channel size.
Tip: Most creators try to monetize everything at once and do all of it poorly. Pick the top 2 revenue streams for your size, master them, then add the next one. Depth beats breadth.
