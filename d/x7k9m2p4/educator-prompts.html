Lesson Planning (1–10)

Prompt 1
Complete Lesson Plan
Create a complete lesson plan for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL], Topic: [TOPIC]. Duration: [MINUTES] minutes. Include the following sections: (1) Learning objectives written using Bloom's taxonomy action verbs, aligned to [STATE/NATIONAL STANDARDS]. (2) Warm-up / bell ringer activity (5 minutes) that activates prior knowledge. (3) Direct instruction (15 minutes) with key vocabulary, guided notes outline, and at least one model/example. (4) Guided practice (10 minutes) where students work with teacher support. (5) Independent practice (10 minutes) with a clear task and success criteria. (6) Closure / exit ticket (5 minutes) that checks for understanding of the lesson objective. Also include: a complete materials list, differentiation notes for ELL students (language scaffolds), advanced learners (extension tasks), and students with IEPs (accommodations). Format the plan so I can print it and hand it to an administrator during a walkthrough.
Tip: Paste your actual state standards code (e.g., CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RL.7.1) for tighter alignment. The more specific your [TOPIC], the more usable the output.
Prompt 2
Unit Plan Builder
Design a [NUMBER]-week unit plan for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Unit theme: [TOPIC]. Use backwards design (Understanding by Design framework) — start with what students should know and be able to do by the end of the unit, then work backwards. Include: (1) 2-3 essential questions that drive inquiry throughout the unit. (2) Enduring understandings students will walk away with. (3) A day-by-day lesson topic sequence with a learning objective for each day. (4) At least 3 formative assessments embedded throughout (describe each briefly). (5) One summative assessment at the end with a description of format and what it measures. (6) Cross-curricular connections to at least 2 other subject areas. (7) Key vocabulary list for the unit. Present the daily sequence in a table format with columns: Day, Topic, Objective, Activity Type, Assessment (if any).
Tip: Specify your school's schedule (block vs. traditional periods) so the pacing is realistic for your actual class time.
Prompt 3
Bell Ringer Generator
Create 20 bell ringer / do-now activities for a [SUBJECT] class, Grade [LEVEL], currently studying [UNIT/TOPIC]. Each activity should take 3-5 minutes and require no materials beyond pencil and paper. Mix the following types across the 20: (1) Review questions from previous lessons. (2) Creative / imaginative prompts related to the content. (3) Real-world connection questions ("Where do you see [CONCEPT] in your daily life?"). (4) Error analysis ("Find and fix the mistake in this example..."). (5) Predict and justify ("What do you think will happen if... and why?"). (6) Quick writes (1-paragraph response to a thought-provoking question). (7) Visual analysis (describe a scenario and ask students to sketch, interpret, or label). Number them 1-20 and label each with its type. Make them progressively build in complexity across the unit so they mirror the learning trajectory.
Tip: Print these and keep them in a binder — you'll have a month of instant warm-ups ready to go. Swap any that don't land for a retry next semester.
Prompt 4
Project-Based Learning Design
Design a project-based learning (PBL) unit for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Driving question: [QUESTION]. Duration: [WEEKS] weeks. Students will work in groups of [NUMBER]. Include: (1) A compelling launch activity that hooks students and introduces the driving question (describe in detail). (2) A project timeline with 4-6 milestones, each with a specific checkpoint deliverable. (3) Defined student roles within each group (e.g., project manager, researcher, designer, presenter) with responsibilities for each. (4) A clear real-world connection — who is the authentic audience? What problem are they solving for a real community, organization, or scenario? (5) Description of the final public product or presentation format. (6) Assessment criteria: a rubric covering both process (collaboration, time management) and product (content accuracy, presentation quality). (7) Scaffolds for groups that get stuck. (8) Individual accountability measures so no one hides behind the group.
Tip: The driving question makes or breaks PBL. If yours feels flat, ask ChatGPT to brainstorm 5 alternatives before committing.
Prompt 5
Substitute Teacher Plans
Write detailed substitute teacher plans for [NUMBER] days of [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Current unit: [TOPIC]. These plans must be so clear that a substitute with zero knowledge of my class can execute them successfully. For each day include: (1) Minute-by-minute schedule with exact times (e.g., "8:00-8:05 — students enter, pick up worksheet from front table, begin bell ringer on the board"). (2) Seating chart reference note (where to find it). (3) Class routines the sub must follow (bathroom policy, phone policy, how to get attention). (4) Full descriptions of all activities — do NOT reference worksheets without describing exactly what's on them. (5) Answer keys for anything students will complete. (6) What to do if students finish early. (7) Behavioral expectations and what to do if a student is disruptive (name of admin to call, room number). (8) Emergency contact info: my email and the department head's name. (9) List of helpful students the sub can rely on (leave a blank for me to fill in).
Tip: Save this as a template and update the content section each time you're out. The routines and procedures part stays the same all year.
Prompt 6
Lab / Activity Instructions
Write clear, step-by-step student instructions for a [SUBJECT] activity: [DESCRIBE ACTIVITY]. Grade [LEVEL]. Format this as a handout I can print and distribute. Include: (1) Activity title and objective stated in student-friendly language ("By the end of this activity, you will be able to..."). (2) Materials list per group. (3) Safety notes and rules (if applicable). (4) Procedure written as numbered steps, each with an estimated time. Use simple, direct language appropriate for Grade [LEVEL] reading level — avoid jargon unless defined. (5) A data collection table or recording sheet built into the instructions. (6) 4-5 analysis questions that move from recall to application (ask students to interpret their results, not just report them). (7) An extension challenge for groups that finish early. (8) A cleanup checklist at the end. Bold any critical safety or procedural warnings.
Tip: Have a colleague read the instructions cold — if they can follow them without asking you a question, your students can too.
Prompt 7
Flipped Classroom Video Guide
I'm flipping my [SUBJECT] class for the topic [TOPIC], Grade [LEVEL]. Create a complete flipped lesson package: (1) A script for a 7-10 minute instructional video. Write it conversationally — as if I'm talking directly to my students, not reading a textbook. Include natural transitions, moments to pause and think, and 2-3 on-screen examples I should show. (2) Three comprehension check questions to embed at specific timestamps in the video (specify where each should appear and what it checks). (3) A guided notes sheet students complete while watching — with blanks to fill in, diagrams to label, and a "questions I still have" section at the bottom. (4) An in-class activity for the next day that applies and deepens the video content through hands-on practice, discussion, or problem-solving. The in-class activity should be impossible to do well without watching the video, creating accountability.
Tip: Record in one take with minor edits. Students prefer authentic over polished — they want to hear your voice, not a production.
Prompt 8
Cross-Curricular Lesson
Design a cross-curricular lesson that meaningfully connects [SUBJECT 1] and [SUBJECT 2] for Grade [LEVEL]. Topic: [TOPIC]. This should not feel forced — show how both subjects genuinely reinforce each other around this topic. Include: (1) A shared learning objective that honors both disciplines. (2) Standards alignment for both [SUBJECT 1] and [SUBJECT 2]. (3) An activity sequence that could be co-taught (both teachers in the room) OR coordinated (taught in separate periods during the same week). Describe both options. (4) Student deliverable that demonstrates competency in both subjects. (5) Assessment approach that both teachers can use. (6) Logistics notes: how to coordinate planning time, shared materials, scheduling considerations. (7) How to pitch this to your colleague and admin — a 2-sentence elevator pitch for each.
Tip: Start small — a single coordinated lesson is more sustainable than an elaborate co-taught unit. Build from success.
Prompt 9
Review Game / Activity
Design an engaging, genuinely fun review activity for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL], covering these topics for an upcoming test: [TOPICS FOR TEST]. Format: [JEOPARDY / ESCAPE ROOM / STATIONS / GALLERY WALK / SCAVENGER HUNT]. Include absolutely everything I need to run this tomorrow: (1) All questions with complete answer keys (at least 25 questions across all topics). (2) Detailed setup instructions and materials needed. (3) Team structure (how many teams, how to form them, roles within teams). (4) Point system and scorekeeping method. (5) Timing for each round or station (total activity: [MINUTES] minutes). (6) Rules for disputes and incorrect answers. (7) Engagement hooks — what makes this actually fun and not just a worksheet disguised as a game? Include competition elements, movement, surprise rounds, or bonus challenges. (8) How to handle the class getting too loud or competitive.
Tip: Test your timing by reading through the questions yourself. If YOU can't answer them all in the allotted time, neither can students.
Prompt 10
Differentiated Lesson Plan
Take this lesson and differentiate it into 3 tiers for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]: [DESCRIBE BASIC LESSON]. All three tiers must target the same core learning objective — students take different paths to get there. Tier 1 (Approaching Grade Level): Provide sentence starters, graphic organizers, simplified or chunked text, vocabulary support, and a model/example to follow. Reduce the number of items but not the rigor of thinking. Tier 2 (At Grade Level): The standard version of the lesson as described, with clear instructions and appropriate challenge. Tier 3 (Above Grade Level): Extend into analysis, evaluation, or creation (upper Bloom's). Add real-world application, open-ended inquiry, or a "what if" scenario that pushes critical thinking beyond the original lesson scope. For each tier, specify: the task, materials/scaffolds, expected output, and how you'll know the student met the objective. Also include a brief note on how to manage all 3 tiers running simultaneously without losing your mind.
Tip: Let students self-select their tier when possible — they often know where they need to be, and choice increases buy-in.

Assessment & Feedback (11–20)

Prompt 11
Rubric Creator
Create a detailed analytic rubric for a [ASSIGNMENT TYPE: essay / project / presentation / lab report] in [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Use 4 performance levels: Exceeds Expectations (4), Meets Expectations (3), Approaching Expectations (2), Beginning (1). Categories to evaluate: [LIST CATEGORIES OR SAY "suggest appropriate categories for this assignment type"]. For each cell in the rubric, write specific, observable, measurable criteria — no vague language like "shows understanding" or "demonstrates knowledge." Instead, describe exactly what the student's work looks like at that level (e.g., "Thesis statement makes a clear, arguable claim and previews 3 supporting points" vs. "Has a thesis"). Include: a row for each category, point values, total points possible, and a notes/comments section. Format as a table. Also provide a student-friendly version that translates the rubric into "I can" statements.
Tip: Share the rubric BEFORE the assignment, not after. Students produce dramatically better work when they know the target.
Prompt 12
Exit Ticket Generator
Create 15 exit tickets for a [SUBJECT] unit on [TOPIC], Grade [LEVEL]. Each exit ticket should take no more than 3 minutes to complete and immediately tell me who understood today's lesson and who didn't. Mix the following types across the 15: (1) Multiple choice with one correct answer and plausible distractors based on common misconceptions (3 tickets). (2) Short answer requiring a 1-2 sentence explanation (3 tickets). (3) Sketch, diagram, or label task (2 tickets). (4) Student self-assessment with justification — "Rate your understanding 1-4 and explain why" (2 tickets). (5) Misconception check — present a common wrong answer and ask "Is this correct? Why or why not?" (3 tickets). (6) Transfer question — apply today's concept to a new, unfamiliar context (2 tickets). Number them 1-15, label each with its type, and note which lesson objective each one assesses. Also include a quick sorting strategy: how do I sort the responses in under 5 minutes to form tomorrow's groups?
Tip: Sort exit tickets into 3 piles as students leave: Got It, Almost, Not Yet. Plan tomorrow's first 10 minutes around the "Not Yet" pile.
Prompt 13
Test / Quiz Creator
Write a [NUMBER]-question assessment for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL], covering these topics: [TOPICS]. Structure: [NUMBER] multiple choice questions (4 answer options each, with plausible distractors drawn from common student misconceptions — not obviously wrong answers), [NUMBER] short answer questions (requiring 2-3 sentences each), and [NUMBER] extended response questions (requiring a paragraph or multi-step solution). Create a complete answer key with point values for each question and a scoring guide for the extended response questions showing what a full-credit, partial-credit, and no-credit answer looks like. Then create a MODIFIED VERSION of the same test for students with testing accommodations: reduce multiple choice to 3 options, provide a word bank for short answer, include a graphic organizer or sentence frame for extended response, and simplify the language without reducing the cognitive demand. Both versions should assess the same standards.
Tip: Review the distractors carefully — they should reflect mistakes real students make, not random wrong answers. This is where AI-generated tests need human editing.
Prompt 14
Feedback Comment Bank
Generate a bank of 30 specific, actionable feedback comments for [ASSIGNMENT TYPE] in [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Organize into three categories of 10 comments each: STRENGTHS (what the student did well — be specific, e.g., "Your use of textual evidence in paragraph 2 directly supports your claim" not "good job"), AREAS FOR GROWTH (what needs improvement — frame constructively, e.g., "Your conclusion restates your thesis but doesn't extend your argument — try adding a 'so what?' statement" not "weak conclusion"), and NEXT STEPS (concrete actions the student can take right now, e.g., "Reread your topic sentences — do they each make a claim, or do some just announce a topic?"). Vary the comments across different skill levels (struggling, proficient, advanced). Also include 5 sentence starters students can use to respond to feedback and revise their work, creating a feedback dialogue rather than a one-way evaluation.
Tip: Copy your favorites into a text expander tool (or just a Google Doc you keep open while grading) so you can paste and personalize in seconds.
Prompt 15
Student Self-Assessment
Create a student self-assessment form for [UNIT/PROJECT] in [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. This should help students genuinely reflect, not just check boxes. Include: (1) A learning target checklist using "I can..." statements derived from the unit objectives. For each target, students rate themselves (not yet / getting there / got it / could teach it) AND cite specific evidence from their work. (2) A reflection section with questions: "What concept or skill was hardest for you, and what did you do when you got stuck?" / "What surprised you about this unit?" / "What would you do differently if you could start over?" / "What are you most proud of in your work, and why?" (3) A goal-setting section for the next unit: one academic goal and one process/habit goal, each with a concrete action step. (4) An optional "message to your teacher" box where students can share anything about their learning experience. Format this as a clean, printable one-page document with enough writing space for each section.
Tip: Do this BEFORE you return graded work. Students' honest self-assessment, compared to your evaluation, reveals metacognitive gaps worth discussing.
Prompt 16
Standards-Based Grade Report
Write narrative report card comments for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Create detailed templates for 3 different student profiles: PROFILE A (Exceeding standards — a student who needs more challenge and is at risk of disengagement): 3-4 sentences highlighting specific strengths with examples, acknowledging growth, and suggesting how the student can push further or take on leadership roles. PROFILE B (Meeting standards — a student showing steady, solid growth): 3-4 sentences affirming consistent effort with specific evidence, noting areas of strongest progress, and identifying one area to sharpen next term. PROFILE C (Approaching standards — a student who needs additional support): 3-4 sentences leading with genuine strengths, honestly naming the gap without discouraging, providing a specific and actionable next step the family can support at home, and expressing confidence in growth. For ALL profiles: use plain language parents understand (no "demonstrates proficiency in standard 4.NF.3"), name the actual skill ("your child can add fractions with like denominators but is still working on unlike denominators"), and end with an encouraging, forward-looking statement.
Tip: Run the final comments through a readability checker — aim for 8th-grade reading level max. Parents skim report cards in under 60 seconds.
Prompt 17
Peer Review Guide
Create a peer review worksheet for [ASSIGNMENT TYPE] in [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. This should guide students to give genuinely useful feedback, not just "it's good." Include: (1) Specific criteria to evaluate, directly tied to the assignment rubric (list 4-6 focus areas). For each criterion, provide a guiding question the reviewer should answer. (2) Sentence starters for constructive feedback organized as: "I noticed..." (observation without judgment), "I wonder..." (questions that push thinking), "Have you considered..." (suggestions). (3) A side-by-side example showing unhelpful feedback vs. helpful feedback for this specific assignment type — make it concrete so students see the difference. (4) A "glow and grow" summary section: one specific strength and one specific suggestion. (5) A revision checklist the author uses after receiving feedback: "Based on my peer's feedback, I will..." with action items. (6) Brief instructions on peer review etiquette (be honest AND kind, critique the work not the person, ask clarifying questions before suggesting changes).
Tip: Model the peer review process live with a sample paper before students do it independently. Five minutes of modeling saves twenty minutes of "I don't know what to write."
Prompt 18
Data Analysis Template
I have these class assessment results for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]: [DESCRIBE DATA OR PASTE SCORES — e.g., "25 students, scores ranged from 42% to 98%, class average 71%, questions covered 4 standards"]. Analyze this data and provide: (1) Statistical summary: class average, median, score distribution (how many in each grade band), standard deviation if useful. (2) Item analysis: which questions or standards were mastered (80%+ correct) vs. not mastered (below 60% correct). Identify the 3 most-missed questions/standards. (3) Student grouping: suggest 3-4 flexible groups based on performance patterns (not just "high/medium/low" — group by specific skill gaps). (4) Reteach plan: for the 2-3 standards that need reteaching, suggest a different instructional approach than what was originally used. (5) Enrichment plan: for students who mastered all standards, suggest meaningful extension activities (not just "more of the same"). (6) A one-page data summary I could share with my PLC or administrator showing results and action plan.
Tip: Anonymize student names before pasting scores into ChatGPT. Use initials or numbers — protect student data always.
Prompt 19
Portfolio Assessment Design
Design a student portfolio assessment system for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL], covering [SEMESTER/YEAR]. Include: (1) Required artifacts list — specify 8-12 pieces of work students must include, representing different skills, formats, and points in the learning timeline. For each artifact, describe what it demonstrates. (2) A reflection prompt for each artifact: "Why did you choose this piece? What does it show about your growth? What would you change?" (3) Portfolio organization structure: sections, table of contents template, cover page requirements. (4) A mid-point check-in protocol where students review their portfolio progress with the teacher (describe the conference format, 5-7 minutes each). (5) Presentation day format: how long each student presents, audience (peers? parents? panel?), presentation requirements, audience feedback form. (6) Evaluation criteria: rubric for both portfolio quality and presentation. (7) Implementation notes: digital platform recommendations vs. physical binder pros/cons, storage logistics, how to manage 30+ portfolios without drowning.
Tip: Start with just 4 required artifacts in your first semester. You can always add more once the system is running — overbuilding kills portfolios.
Prompt 20
Formative Assessment Toolkit
Give me 20 quick formative assessment strategies for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL] that don't feel like tests to students. Organize into 5 categories with 4 strategies each: MOVEMENT-BASED (students physically move to show understanding — e.g., four corners, human number line), TECH-BASED (using devices students already have — e.g., quick polls, shared docs, QR code responses), DISCUSSION-BASED (structured talk protocols — e.g., think-pair-share variations, Socratic fishbowl, philosophical chairs), WRITING-BASED (quick writes that reveal thinking — e.g., 3-2-1, muddiest point, explain to a younger student), VISUAL-BASED (drawing, mapping, or organizing ideas — e.g., sketch notes, concept maps, before/after diagrams). For each strategy include: (a) description in 2-3 sentences, (b) when to use it in a lesson (beginning, middle, or end), (c) what data it gives you and how to act on it, (d) estimated time needed, (e) one concrete example specific to [SUBJECT].
Tip: Pick 3-4 favorites from this list and rotate them consistently. Students get better at formative assessment when the format is familiar — the content is the variable, not the method.

Communication (21–28)

Prompt 21
Parent Email Templates
Write 5 parent/guardian email templates for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Each should be professional, warm, and solutions-oriented. Include a subject line for each. (1) POSITIVE UPDATE: sharing a specific achievement or behavior worth celebrating. Not generic — reference something the student actually did. End with a question that invites the parent to respond. (2) ACADEMIC CONCERN: a student's grade is dropping. Lead with something positive, then clearly state the concern with specific data (missing assignments, assessment scores). Propose a concrete plan and ask for their input. (3) BEHAVIORAL ISSUE (first contact): describe the behavior factually without labeling the student. Explain the impact on learning, what you've already tried, and ask the parent for their perspective — they know their child better than you do. (4) MEETING REQUEST: requesting a conference (in-person or virtual). Give 3 time options, briefly state the purpose, and reassure the parent this is collaborative, not punitive. (5) END-OF-TERM SUMMARY: a warm wrap-up covering growth areas, highlights, and what's ahead next term. Each email should be under 200 words — parents don't read long emails.
Tip: Always CC your school email (for documentation) and never put anything in an email you wouldn't want read aloud at a board meeting.
Prompt 22
Newsletter / Update Template
Write a [WEEKLY/MONTHLY] classroom newsletter template for Grade [LEVEL], [SUBJECT]. This should feel like a real person wrote it — informative but personal, not robotic or overly formal. Sections: (1) WHAT WE LEARNED: 2-3 sentence summary of key topics covered, written so a parent who knows nothing about [SUBJECT] can understand. (2) WHAT'S COMING UP: preview of next week's/month's topics and any major assignments or assessments with dates. (3) HOW TO HELP AT HOME: 1-2 specific, realistic suggestions (not "review notes nightly" — something a busy parent can actually do, like "Ask your child to explain what a metaphor is and find one in a song they like"). (4) STUDENT SPOTLIGHT: a rotating feature celebrating effort, improvement, or kindness (leave a blank for the name). (5) IMPORTANT DATES: bulleted list with dates bolded. (6) TEACHER NOTE: a brief, human moment — something funny that happened in class, what you're excited about teaching next, or a thank-you. Keep the whole newsletter to one printed page. Suggest a free tool for making it visually appealing.
Tip: Send it on the same day each week so families build a habit of reading it. Consistency beats perfection — a short newsletter sent is better than a beautiful one never finished.
Prompt 23
IEP Meeting Prep
Help me prepare for an IEP meeting for a student in [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. Student's primary challenges: [DESCRIBE]. Current accommodations in my class: [LIST]. Data I have: [ASSESSMENT RESULTS, GRADES, BEHAVIORAL OBSERVATIONS]. Help me with: (1) Organizing my observations into a clear, concise "present levels of performance" draft — academic performance, functional performance, and how the disability affects involvement in the general curriculum. Use objective, data-driven language. (2) Drafting 2-3 measurable annual goals following the format: "By [DATE], given [CONDITIONS], [STUDENT] will [BEHAVIOR] as measured by [CRITERIA] with [ACCURACY]%." (3) Recommending specific accommodations and/or modifications that are practical and enforceable in my classroom (don't suggest things I can't realistically do with 30 students). (4) Anticipating questions the parent might ask and preparing thoughtful, honest responses. (5) Listing strengths and positive observations to lead with — IEP meetings should not feel like a deficit inventory. (6) Questions I should ask the parent, the special education teacher, and the student (if age-appropriate to attend).
Tip: Review this with your special education co-teacher before the meeting. They can catch compliance issues and strengthen your goal language.
Prompt 24
Student Recommendation Letter
Write a recommendation letter for [STUDENT NAME — use a placeholder] who is applying to [COLLEGE / PROGRAM / SCHOLARSHIP / AWARD]. I teach [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL], and have known this student for [DURATION]. Student's academic strengths: [LIST]. Specific memorable examples: [DESCRIBE 2-3 MOMENTS — e.g., "led a class discussion on climate policy where they respectfully challenged a peer's data," "stayed after school for 3 weeks to master polynomial division," "organized a study group without being asked"]. Character traits I'd highlight: [LIST — e.g., resilience, intellectual curiosity, quiet leadership, humor]. Write the letter in a warm, specific, credible voice. Avoid generic superlatives ("best student I've ever had") — instead, show through stories and concrete examples. The letter should be 400-500 words, addressed to "Dear Admissions Committee" (or "[SPECIFIC RECIPIENT]"), and formatted as a formal letter with my sign-off as [YOUR NAME], [YOUR TITLE], [SCHOOL NAME]. Admissions officers read thousands of these — make this one memorable.
Tip: Ask the student to fill out a "brag sheet" listing their activities, goals, and moments they're proud of. You'll discover things you didn't know and can reference them.
Prompt 25
Difficult Conversation Script
I need to have a difficult conversation with a [PARENT / STUDENT / COLLEAGUE / ADMINISTRATOR] about [ISSUE]. Context: [PROVIDE DETAILS — what happened, what you've observed, why it matters, any history]. Help me prepare: (1) An opening statement that is direct but not confrontational — name the issue without triggering defensiveness. Give me the exact words to say. (2) 3-4 factual observations I can present objectively, without interpretation or judgment (what I saw/heard vs. what I think it means). (3) Active listening questions to understand their perspective: "Help me understand..." / "What's your experience of this?" / "What am I missing?" (4) A collaborative problem-solving section: "Here's what I'd like to propose..." with 2-3 possible solutions that give the other person agency and choice. (5) A closing that summarizes agreements and next steps. (6) Documentation template: date, attendees, key points discussed, agreed-upon actions, follow-up date. Also give me: phrases to AVOID (accusatory, absolute, or inflammatory language) and phrases to USE INSTEAD. Prepare me for the worst-case response and how to stay calm and redirect.
Tip: Practice saying your opening statement out loud 3 times before the meeting. It always sounds different out of your mouth than it does in your head.
Prompt 26
Classroom Policy Document
Write a classroom expectations and policies document for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. This is the document students and families receive at the start of the year. Write it TO students, not about them — use "you" and "we." Include: (1) A welcoming opening (2-3 sentences about what excites you about this course and what students can expect). (2) Course description: what students will learn and why it matters. (3) Grading policy: categories and weights, what counts as an A/B/C, how I use formative vs. summative assessments. (4) Late work policy: be clear and specific — how many days, what penalty if any, how to communicate about extensions. (5) Technology policy: when devices are welcome, when they're away, consequences for misuse. (6) Behavior expectations framed positively: "In this class, we..." rather than "Don't..." (7) How to reach me: email, office hours, response time expectations. (8) What to do if you're struggling — specific steps before panic sets in. (9) Academic integrity statement that educates rather than threatens. Keep the tone firm but warm. Format cleanly with headers and bullets. One page front and back, maximum.
Tip: Read this aloud from a student's perspective. If it sounds like a list of punishments, revise until it sounds like an invitation to learn.
Prompt 27
Back-to-School Night Presentation
Write a script and slide outline for a [10/15/20]-minute Back-to-School Night presentation for [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL]. This is for parents, not students. Include: (1) Introduction (1 minute): who I am — brief, personal, relatable. Not my full resume. One sentence about why I love teaching [SUBJECT]. (2) Course overview (3 minutes): what their child will learn this year, described in parent-friendly terms. Show the arc of the year, not a list of standards. (3) A "day in the life" snapshot (2 minutes): walk parents through what a typical class period looks like so they can picture their child in the room. (4) Grading and assessment (3 minutes): how grades work, where to check grades online, when major assessments happen, and how to interpret scores. (5) How to support learning at home (2 minutes): 3 specific, realistic actions. (6) Communication (2 minutes): how and when to reach me, response time expectations, newsletter schedule. (7) Close and Q&A (remaining time): end with the ONE thing you want parents to remember. Suggest questions to prompt participation if the room is quiet. Tone: confident, warm, and organized — not reading slides word-for-word. Note where to make eye contact and pause.
Tip: Have a sign-in sheet with parent email addresses at the door. You'll use it all year, and it shows you're organized before you even start talking.
Prompt 28
Grant / Funding Request
Write a compelling grant application or funding request letter for [PROJECT/RESOURCE] for my [SUBJECT] class, Grade [LEVEL]. Total budget needed: [AMOUNT]. Include: (1) Project title and one-sentence hook. (2) Statement of need: what problem does this solve? Use specific data — how many students are affected, what's the current gap, what's the consequence of doing nothing? (3) Project description: what exactly will you do? Timeline, activities, and who's involved. Be specific enough that a funder can picture the project in action. (4) Student impact: how many students benefit, what will change in their learning experience? Include qualitative outcomes (engagement, confidence) and quantitative goals (assessment score improvement, participation rates). (5) Alignment to school/district strategic goals — connect your project to priorities they've already stated. (6) Detailed budget breakdown: itemized list with costs, quantities, and justification for each line item. No padding — funders spot inflated budgets. (7) Sustainability plan: how will this continue after the grant period? (8) Assessment plan: how will you measure and report success? Include a timeline for reporting. Write in a tone that is passionate but evidence-based — show you've done your homework.
Tip: Call the grant organization before applying to ask what makes a strong application. This also puts your name in their mind before they read your proposal.

Classroom Management & Professional (29–35)

Prompt 29
Seating Chart Strategy
I have [NUMBER] students in my [SUBJECT] class, Grade [LEVEL]. Here are the key considerations for seating: [LIST — e.g., "3 students who are best friends and chat nonstop when near each other," "2 students who need to sit near the front due to vision/hearing needs," "1 student on a behavior intervention plan who needs proximity to the teacher," "4 high-achieving students who could serve as peer mentors," "2 students who had a conflict last semester and should be separated," "5 students who are ELL and benefit from sitting near bilingual peers"]. Suggest 3 different seating arrangements, each with a clear rationale: (A) Traditional rows — when would this be best and how would you arrange these specific students? (B) Groups of 4 — which students would you group together and why? (C) U-shape or horseshoe — advantages for discussion-based lessons and placement strategy. For each arrangement, explain: where specific student types sit, sightline considerations, traffic flow for the teacher to circulate, and which arrangement works best for which type of lesson. Include a note on how often to change seats and how to handle student complaints about their assigned seat.
Tip: Change seats every 3-4 weeks. Announce it cheerfully ("New seats — new neighbors — fresh start!"). Students adjust faster than you expect.
Prompt 30
Behavior Management Plan
Create an individualized behavior management plan for a student in [SUBJECT], Grade [LEVEL] who is displaying these behaviors: [DESCRIBE — e.g., calling out without raising hand, leaving seat frequently, refusing to start work, arguing with peers, shutting down when corrected]. Include: (1) ANTECEDENT STRATEGIES (prevent the behavior before it starts): environmental changes, seating, task modifications, pre-corrective statements, visual cues, check-in routines. List 5-6 specific strategies. (2) REPLACEMENT BEHAVIORS to explicitly teach the student: what should they do INSTEAD? Don't just say "stop calling out" — teach an alternative that meets the same need. (3) POSITIVE REINFORCEMENT PLAN: what specific, meaningful reinforcers will you use? How frequently? Include a simple tracking system (point card, token board, or check-in/check-out sheet) that takes less than 30 seconds to update because I am actively teaching 29 other students. (4) CONSEQUENCE LADDER: progressive, predictable, respectful consequences when the behavior occurs. Not punitive — logical. (5) DATA COLLECTION method: a simple frequency count or interval recording sheet I can manage during instruction. (6) PARENT COMMUNICATION plan: how and how often to update the family, sample email/note for positive reports and concerns. (7) Review timeline: when to evaluate if the plan is working and criteria for adjusting.
Tip: The reinforcement must be something the STUDENT values, not what you think they should value. Ask them. A 2-minute chat with a favorite staff member might outweigh a sticker chart.
Prompt 31
Morning Meeting / Advisory Plan
Create [4/5] weeks of daily morning meeting or advisory plans for Grade [LEVEL]. Each session is 15 minutes and follows this structure: Greeting (2 min), Share (3 min), Activity (7 min), Daily Message (3 min). Rotate weekly themes: Week 1 — Community Building (getting to know each other, norms, trust), Week 2 — Social-Emotional Learning (emotions, empathy, conflict resolution), Week 3 — Academic Preview & Study Skills (goal setting, organization, test prep strategies), Week 4 — Current Events & Critical Thinking (age-appropriate topics, media literacy), Week 5 (if applicable) — Fun & Team Building (brain breaks, challenges, celebrations). For each day, provide: the specific greeting format, a share prompt or question, a detailed activity description (not just "play a game" — describe the game, rules, and materials), and the daily message content. Activities should require minimal materials and work in a standard classroom. Include at least 3 activities that get students out of their seats. Make it a space students actually look forward to, not another obligation.
Tip: The greeting sets the tone for everything. If students won't do a silly greeting, they're not ready for vulnerable sharing. Build up gradually.
Prompt 32
Professional Development Workshop
Design a [30/60/90]-minute professional development workshop for teachers on [TOPIC]. I'm facilitating this for [AUDIENCE — e.g., my department, whole staff, new teachers]. Include: (1) Hook / warm-up (first 5-7 minutes): an engaging opening that immediately models the strategy or demonstrates the problem. NOT an icebreaker for the sake of an icebreaker — it should connect directly to the content. (2) Content delivery (15-20 minutes): key concepts with a model lesson or demonstration. Show, don't tell. Weave in relevant research briefly — teachers want the "why" but not a literature review. (3) Participant practice time (15-20 minutes): hands-on application where teachers work with their own content, curriculum, or students in mind. Provide a template or planning tool they fill out during this time. (4) Collaborative sharing (10 minutes): structured protocol for sharing what they created or discussed — not open-ended "anyone want to share?" (5) Takeaway: a one-page resource or template they can use in their classroom tomorrow. Not next month — tomorrow. Include facilitator notes throughout (what to say, when to transition, how to handle the skeptic in the room, time checks). End with a feedback form: 3 questions max.
Tip: The #1 complaint about PD is "this doesn't apply to me." Build in choice points where teachers adapt the content to their own subject and grade level.
Prompt 33
Classroom Routine Procedures
Write detailed classroom procedures for a [SUBJECT] classroom, Grade [LEVEL]. But don't just list the rules — write a plan for HOW TO TEACH each procedure during the first week of school. For each of the following routines, provide: the procedure itself (step-by-step), how to explicitly teach it (model, practice, reinforce), and what to do when a student doesn't follow it. Routines to cover: (1) Entering the room and starting the bell ringer. (2) Transitioning between activities (what does "switch" look like?). (3) Getting teacher attention / asking a question during work time. (4) Bathroom and water break policy. (5) Group work: roles, voice levels, how to disagree respectfully. (6) Packing up and dismissal (teacher dismisses, not the bell). (7) Fire drill / lockdown procedures specific to your room. (8) Technology use: when devices come out, where they go when not in use, what happens if misused. (9) What to do when you finish early. (10) What to do when you're absent (how to find what you missed). For each, estimate how many minutes to spend teaching it on Day 1, and when to reteach (after a break, when it starts slipping). Include a "first week schedule" showing when to teach each procedure.
Tip: Practice the procedures. Literally. Have students walk in, sit down, start the bell ringer, and then do it again until it's smooth. This is not wasted time — it's the most valuable time you'll spend all year.
Prompt 34
Student Interest Survey
Create a beginning-of-year student survey for Grade [LEVEL] that actually helps me teach them better. Keep it genuine, not cheesy — students can spot performative "getting to know you" activities instantly. Sections: (1) HOW YOU LEARN BEST: Instead of outdated "learning styles," ask practical questions: "Do you prefer working alone, in pairs, or in groups — and why?" / "When you're stuck, what do you usually do first?" / "Do you like being called on in class, or does that stress you out?" (2) YOUR LIFE OUTSIDE SCHOOL: interests, hobbies, after-school commitments, part-time job, family responsibilities. One question: "Is there anything happening outside school that might affect your school year?" (3) YOUR EXPERIENCE WITH [SUBJECT]: "What's your honest feeling about [SUBJECT] on a scale of 'love it' to 'dread it'? No wrong answer." / "Name one thing a past teacher did that actually helped you learn [SUBJECT]" / "What's one thing past teachers did that didn't help?" (4) THIS YEAR: "What do you want to get out of this class?" / "How do you prefer to show what you know — test, project, presentation, paper, conversation?" / "Is there anything you want me to know about you that I wouldn't learn from your transcript?" Keep it to one page. Include a note at the top: "Your answers are between you and me. Be honest — it helps me teach you better."
Tip: Actually read every single one. Then reference a student's answer in conversation within the first two weeks — "You mentioned you're into robotics — this physics concept connects to that." Trust is built in the small moments.
Prompt 35
Teacher Self-Care Plan
I'm a [SUBJECT] teacher, Grade [LEVEL], in my [NUMBER] year of teaching. Right now I'm feeling [BURNED OUT / OVERWHELMED / UNMOTIVATED / ISOLATED / ALL OF THE ABOVE]. Help me create a realistic, sustainable self-care plan — not bubble baths and inspirational quotes, but actual structural changes. Include: (1) DAILY MICRO-PRACTICES (5 minutes or less): 3-4 things I can do between classes or during planning that genuinely reset my nervous system. Be specific. (2) WEEKLY BOUNDARIES: 2-3 boundaries to set and protect — e.g., a hard stop time for work, a no-email window, one evening that is fully school-free. Help me script how to communicate these boundaries without guilt. (3) GRADING EFFICIENCY: hacks specific to [SUBJECT] that cut grading time in half without reducing feedback quality. Be practical — rubric shortcuts, batch feedback methods, which assignments to grade deeply vs. completion-only. (4) ONE THING TO STOP DOING: identify a task I'm probably doing that isn't actually required or effective, and give me permission (and a script) to let it go. (5) PROFESSIONAL COMMUNITY: how to find or build connection with other teachers — online communities, local meetups, or in-building allies. Specific suggestions, not "find your people." (6) REALITY CHECK: what does "good enough" teaching actually look like? Write a compassionate but honest paragraph about sustainable teaching vs. martyr teaching. The goal is a career, not a sprint.
Tip: You cannot pour from an empty cup, and you cannot teach well while running on fumes. Taking care of yourself IS taking care of your students. This is not selfish — it is strategic.
