Real Estate Agent's AI Toolkit: 30 ChatGPT Prompts

For ChatGPT or Claude — Listings, clients, marketing, market analysis. Close more deals with AI.

Property Listing Descriptions

PROMPT 01
Luxury Property Listing
Write a luxury real estate listing description for the property at [PROPERTY ADDRESS] in [NEIGHBORHOOD/CITY]. The home has [BEDROOMS] bedrooms, [BATHROOMS] bathrooms, [SQUARE FOOTAGE] square feet, and is listed at [PRICE]. Key luxury features include: [LIST 3-5 PREMIUM FEATURES, e.g., chef's kitchen with Sub-Zero appliances, heated infinity pool, smart home automation, wine cellar, primary suite with spa bathroom]. The lot is [LOT SIZE] and the home was built in [YEAR BUILT]. Write in an aspirational, sophisticated tone that appeals to high-net-worth buyers. Open with an evocative one-line hook. Highlight the lifestyle the home offers, not just the features. Weave in the prestige of the neighborhood. Keep the description between 200 and 300 words. End with a compelling call to action for a private showing.
Tip: Replace the bracketed placeholders with real details before pasting into ChatGPT. The more specific you are about luxury features, the better the output.
PROMPT 02
First-Time Buyer Friendly Listing
Write a warm, inviting property listing description aimed at first-time home buyers for the property at [PROPERTY ADDRESS]. It has [BEDROOMS] bedrooms, [BATHROOMS] bathrooms, [SQUARE FOOTAGE] sq ft, listed at [PRICE]. Features include: [LIST KEY FEATURES, e.g., updated kitchen, fenced backyard, attached garage, new HVAC]. The home is in [NEIGHBORHOOD], which is known for [NEIGHBORHOOD HIGHLIGHTS, e.g., walkability, good schools, parks nearby, low crime rate]. Write the listing in a friendly, approachable tone that avoids jargon. Emphasize move-in readiness, low maintenance, and value for money. Mention proximity to [NEARBY AMENITIES like schools, shopping, transit]. Address common first-time buyer concerns by subtly highlighting things like a newer roof, updated electrical, or home warranty eligibility. Keep it between 150 and 250 words. End with encouragement to schedule a showing and mention the listing agent is happy to walk them through the buying process.

PROMPT 08
Rewrite a Boring MLS Description into Compelling Copy
I have a bland, generic MLS listing description that I need you to completely rewrite into compelling, emotionally engaging marketing copy. Here is the original description: "[PASTE THE ORIGINAL MLS DESCRIPTION HERE]". The property is at [PROPERTY ADDRESS], listed at [PRICE], in the [NEIGHBORHOOD] area. Rewrite this description with the following guidelines: (1) Open with a captivating hook that stops the scroll — avoid cliches like "Welcome home" or "Don't miss this one." (2) Transform generic feature lists into lifestyle benefits — don't just say "granite countertops," paint a picture of entertaining friends around the kitchen island. (3) Highlight the three strongest selling points and make them vivid. (4) Weave in neighborhood appeal and proximity to desirable amenities. (5) Use sensory language that helps buyers imagine living there. (6) End with a specific, urgent call to action. Keep the rewrite between 200 and 300 words. Maintain factual accuracy — don't invent features that aren't in the original. Output the rewrite only, no commentary.

Client Communication

PROMPT 09
First Meeting Follow-Up Email — Buyer
Write a follow-up email from a real estate agent to a buyer after their first consultation meeting. The buyer's name is [CLIENT NAME]. We met on [DATE] at [LOCATION, e.g., my office, a coffee shop, virtually on Zoom]. Their key criteria include: budget of [BUDGET], looking in [TARGET NEIGHBORHOODS], need [BEDROOMS] bedrooms and [BATHROOMS] bathrooms, and their timeline is [TIMELINE, e.g., 3 months, ASAP, flexible]. Special requirements or priorities they mentioned: [SPECIAL NOTES, e.g., home office space, large yard for dogs, near good schools, single-story for accessibility]. Write the email in a professional yet warm and personal tone. The email should: (1) Thank them for their time and express genuine excitement about helping them. (2) Recap their criteria to show I was listening carefully. (3) Briefly outline the next steps I'll take, such as setting up an MLS search, scheduling viewings, or getting them pre-approved. (4) Include one helpful tip or resource for first-time or current buyers. (5) End with a clear next action and my contact info. Keep it concise — under 250 words. Sign it from [YOUR NAME], [YOUR TITLE], [YOUR BROKERAGE], [YOUR PHONE], [YOUR EMAIL].
Tip: Send this within 2 hours of the meeting. Speed and personalization set you apart. The more details you fill in, the more genuine it sounds.

PROMPT 11
Offer Rejection Consolation Email
Write a compassionate, encouraging email from a real estate agent to a buyer client whose offer was not accepted. The buyer's name is [CLIENT NAME]. They offered [OFFER AMOUNT] on [PROPERTY ADDRESS], but the seller went with another offer. The reason, if known, is: [REASON, e.g., a higher offer came in, seller chose an all-cash offer, the other buyer waived inspection contingency, multiple offer situation]. Write this email with empathy first — acknowledge their disappointment, especially if they were emotionally attached to this home. Then pivot to a forward-looking, optimistic tone. The email should: (1) Validate their feelings without being patronizing. (2) Briefly explain the situation and, if applicable, why it wasn't a reflection of their offer strength. (3) Reassure them that the right home is still ahead and that we'll be even more strategically prepared next time. (4) Mention one or two other properties currently on the market or coming soon that might interest them, or say I'm actively searching. (5) Offer a specific next step, like scheduling viewings this weekend. Keep it under 200 words. Tone should be warm, genuine, and motivating — not corporate or scripted.
Tip: This email can make or break a client relationship. A genuine, empathetic response here builds trust and loyalty for the long haul.
PROMPT 12
Price Reduction Conversation Script
Write a phone call or in-person conversation script for a real estate agent recommending a price reduction to a seller client. The seller is [CLIENT NAME]. Their property at [PROPERTY ADDRESS] has been on the market for [DAYS ON MARKET] days at [CURRENT PRICE]. We have had [NUMBER OF SHOWINGS] showings and [NUMBER OF OFFERS] offers so far. Feedback from showing agents includes: [FEEDBACK SUMMARY, e.g., buyers love the layout but feel it's overpriced for the area, comparable homes are selling for less, buyers mention the kitchen needs updating]. I am recommending a price reduction to [RECOMMENDED NEW PRICE], based on recent comparable sales: [LIST 2-3 COMPS with addresses and sale prices]. Write the script in a diplomatic, data-driven, empathetic tone. The script should: (1) Open by checking in on how they're feeling about the process. (2) Present the market data objectively — showings, feedback, days on market, and comps. (3) Frame the price reduction as a strategic move, not a defeat. (4) Explain what typically happens after a well-timed price adjustment — renewed interest, new buyer pool, algorithm re-boost on Zillow/Realtor.com. (5) Anticipate and address common objections like "let's just wait" or "our neighbor sold for more." (6) End with a recommended next step and timeline. Format as a natural conversation with suggested talking points, not a robotic script.
PROMPT 13
Open House Follow-Up Email Sequence (3 Emails)
Write a 3-email follow-up sequence for leads collected at an open house. The property is [PROPERTY ADDRESS], listed at [PRICE], with [BEDROOMS] bedrooms, [BATHROOMS] bathrooms, [SQUARE FOOTAGE] sq ft. My name is [YOUR NAME] with [YOUR BROKERAGE]. Email 1 — Send same day: A warm thank-you for visiting. Mention one standout feature of the home. Ask if they have any questions or would like to schedule a private showing. Include a link to the full listing. Keep it brief and personal (under 125 words). Email 2 — Send 3 days later: Provide a helpful market insight about the [NEIGHBORHOOD] area — maybe a stat about appreciation, days on market, or new development. Mention that I can help them find similar homes if this one wasn't the right fit. Position myself as a neighborhood expert. Include a soft call to action (under 150 words). Email 3 — Send 7 days later: Final touchpoint. Mention any updates on the listing (price change, offer deadline, high interest). Offer a free buyer consultation or home value assessment if they're also thinking of selling. Include a direct call to action with phone number. Create a sense of helpful urgency without being pushy (under 150 words). Write all three emails in a warm, professional tone. Label each clearly as Email 1, Email 2, and Email 3 with subject lines.
Tip: Load these into your CRM as a drip sequence and you'll never lose an open house lead again.

PROMPT 15
Objection Handling Scripts — Common Buyer & Seller Objections
Write professional, persuasive response scripts for the following common real estate objections. For each objection, provide a 3-4 sentence response that is empathetic, logical, and redirects toward action. Use a consultative, non-pushy tone throughout. The objections to address are: (1) Buyer: "We want to wait for prices to drop before buying." (2) Buyer: "We found a home we like on Zillow — can we just contact the listing agent directly?" (3) Buyer: "Your commission is too high. Another agent offered to rebate part of theirs." (4) Seller: "We want to price it higher and leave room for negotiation." (5) Seller: "Let's just try it at this price for a month and see what happens." (6) Seller: "My neighbor's home sold for more, so mine should too." (7) Seller: "We don't want to do repairs or staging — we're selling as-is." (8) Both: "We want to think about it and get back to you." For each response, acknowledge the client's concern first, then provide data or logic, then suggest a next step. Format as: Objection → Response. Make the responses sound natural and conversational, not rehearsed or aggressive.
Tip: Print these out and keep them at your desk. Practice saying them out loud until they feel natural — that's when they become truly effective.

Structure the newsletter as follows: (1) A friendly, conversational opening paragraph that summarizes the market in plain English — is it a buyer's market, seller's market, or balanced? (4) A "What This Means for Sellers" paragraph with actionable advice. Tone should be authoritative but accessible — you're the trusted local expert, not a boring economist.
Tip: Pull your local stats from your MLS or Realtor association reports. Even rough numbers will produce a solid newsletter you can refine.

PROMPT 23
Expired Listing Prospecting Letter
Write a compelling prospecting letter from a real estate agent to a homeowner whose listing just expired without selling. The property is at [PROPERTY ADDRESS]. It was previously listed at [PREVIOUS LIST PRICE] with [PREVIOUS AGENT/BROKERAGE if known, or "another brokerage"]. It was on the market for [DAYS ON MARKET] days. My name is [YOUR NAME] with [YOUR BROKERAGE], and I specialize in the [NEIGHBORHOOD/AREA] market. Write this letter with the following approach: (1) Open with empathy — acknowledge that having a home sit on the market without selling is frustrating and that they may be feeling disappointed or unsure about next steps. Do NOT trash the previous agent. (2) Establish credibility — mention my recent sales in the area: [LIST 1-2 RECENT COMPARABLE SALES, e.g., "I recently sold 123 Oak St in 14 days at 98% of list price"]. Tone should be professional, empathetic, and confident — not desperate or salesy. Format as a physical letter (with date, address block, greeting, body, sign-off).
Tip: Mail this within 48 hours of expiration. Follow up with a phone call 3 days after mailing. The combination of letter + call dramatically improves response rates.
