12 ChatGPT Prompts Every Small Business Owner Needs (2026)

March 6, 2026 · 10 min read · By Piet Suess

Small business owners wear every hat. You are the marketing team, the strategist, the copywriter, the operations manager, and the person who fixes the printer. ChatGPT can shoulder a surprising amount of that load — but only if you give it the right prompts. Most people type "write me a blog post" and wonder why the output reads like it was written by a committee of no one. The difference between useless AI output and genuinely valuable work comes down to prompt engineering: giving ChatGPT enough context, structure, and constraints to produce something you can actually use. These 12 prompts are not vague suggestions. They are complete, tested, copy-paste-ready instructions that produce real deliverables for your business. Each one is built around a specific framework, asks for specific outputs, and includes the kind of context that makes AI for small business actually work.

Marketing Prompts

1 Landing Page Hero Copy (PAS Framework)

You are a direct-response copywriter with 15 years of experience writing landing pages for small businesses. Write landing page hero copy for my business using the Problem-Agitation-Solution (PAS) framework. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS IN 2-3 SENTENCES] My target customer: [WHO THEY ARE, WHAT THEY STRUGGLE WITH] My main offer: [YOUR PRODUCT OR SERVICE] My key differentiator: [WHAT MAKES YOU DIFFERENT FROM COMPETITORS] Deliver the following: 1. A headline (max 10 words) that names the customer's core pain 2. A sub-headline (max 25 words) that agitates the problem with a specific, relatable scenario 3. Three bullet points that present your solution as the obvious answer — each bullet starts with a concrete outcome, not a feature 4. A CTA button label (max 5 words) and one line of supporting text beneath it that reduces risk (e.g., guarantee, no commitment, free trial) 5. An alternative headline and sub-headline option with a completely different emotional angle Format the output with clear labels. Write in a conversational, confident tone. Avoid jargon, hype words like "revolutionary," and generic phrases like "take your business to the next level."

This works because it constrains ChatGPT to a proven copywriting framework instead of letting it freestyle. The specificity of asking for word counts, concrete outcomes over features, and a second angle gives you genuinely usable options. Customize the four bracketed fields with as much detail as you can — the more context, the sharper the output.

2 Email Welcome Sequence

You are an email marketing strategist who specializes in small business welcome sequences. Write a 5-email welcome sequence for new subscribers to my business. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS] What subscribers signed up for: [LEAD MAGNET, NEWSLETTER, PURCHASE, ETC.] My target audience: [WHO THEY ARE AND WHAT THEY CARE ABOUT] My voice/tone: [E.G., FRIENDLY AND DIRECT, PROFESSIONAL BUT WARM] Structure each email with: - Email number and send timing (e.g., Email 1: Immediately, Email 2: Day 2) - Subject line (write 2 options per email, one curiosity-driven, one benefit-driven) - Preview text (the snippet that shows in inbox) - Full email body (150-250 words each) - Specific goal for each email The sequence goals should follow this progression: - Email 1: Deliver the promised value + set expectations for future emails - Email 2: Tell your origin story + build trust through vulnerability - Email 3: Provide a genuinely useful tip that delivers a quick win - Email 4: Share social proof (customer result, testimonial framework, or case study) - Email 5: Make a soft offer with a clear, low-pressure CTA Write in a way that sounds like a real person, not a marketing automation. No "Hey [FIRST NAME]!" openings. Each email should be complete and ready to load into any email platform.

The progressive goal structure is what makes this valuable. Most welcome sequences either sell too hard too fast or never sell at all. By defining the emotional arc across all five emails and specifying word counts, you get a complete, balanced sequence. Swap in your real origin story for Email 2 to make it authentic.

3 Social Media Content Calendar

You are a social media strategist for small businesses with limited time and no dedicated marketing team. Create a 30-day social media content calendar for my business. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS] Platforms I use: [E.G., INSTAGRAM, LINKEDIN, TIKTOK] My audience: [WHO FOLLOWS ME AND WHAT THEY CARE ABOUT] Content I can realistically produce: [E.G., PHOTOS OF MY WORK, BEHIND-THE-SCENES, TALKING HEAD VIDEOS, TEXT POSTS] Business goal this month: [E.G., DRIVE TRAFFIC TO WEBSITE, BOOK DISCOVERY CALLS, LAUNCH A PRODUCT] For each of the 30 days, provide: - Platform(s) to post on - Content type (carousel, single image, reel/short video, text post, story) - Topic/hook (the specific angle — not "post about your product" but "show the before/after of a recent client project with the specific transformation in the caption") - Caption draft (full caption, ready to post, with appropriate length for the platform) - Hashtag set (5-10 relevant hashtags, mix of broad and niche) - Best posting time Follow the 4-1-1 content ratio: for every 4 value/educational posts, include 1 personal/behind-the-scenes post and 1 promotional post. Never post back-to-back promotional content. Each caption should have a hook in the first line that stops the scroll. Format as a table with columns: Day | Platform | Type | Topic | Caption | Hashtags | Time.

The 4-1-1 ratio prevents the most common small business social media mistake: posting nothing but promotions. By specifying you want full captions ready to post (not just topic ideas), you cut your actual content creation time dramatically. Adjust the platforms and content types to match what you will realistically produce.

Strategy Prompts

4 Competitor Analysis

You are a business strategy consultant conducting a competitor analysis for a small business owner. Analyze my competitive landscape and provide actionable insights. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS, LOCATION, AND WHAT YOU SELL] My top 3-5 competitors: [LIST NAMES AND WEBSITES IF POSSIBLE] What I think my advantage is: [YOUR HONEST ASSESSMENT] My price range: [YOUR PRICING] For each competitor, analyze and present: 1. Positioning: How do they describe themselves? What audience are they targeting? What is their core promise? 2. Pricing: What are their price points (if public)? How does their pricing strategy compare to mine (premium, mid-market, budget)? 3. Online Presence: Website quality, social media activity level, content strategy, SEO visibility (what terms they likely rank for) 4. Strengths: What do they do better than me? Be honest. 5. Weaknesses: Where do they fall short? Look at reviews, gaps in their offering, poor UX, missing features. 6. Customer Sentiment: Based on available reviews and social comments, what do their customers love and complain about? Then provide: - A competitive positioning map showing where each competitor sits on two axes: Price (low to high) and Perceived Quality/Experience (low to high) - 3 specific market gaps or underserved segments I could own - 5 concrete actions I can take in the next 30 days to differentiate, ranked by effort vs. impact Be direct. If a competitor is doing something better than me, say so. I need honesty, not reassurance.

The instruction to "be direct" and "I need honesty, not reassurance" is crucial. ChatGPT defaults to positive, diplomatic language. This prompt overrides that tendency. The 30-day action list ranked by effort vs. impact turns analysis into something you can act on Monday morning.

5 90-Day Sprint Plan

You are a business coach who specializes in helping small business owners and entrepreneurs execute focused 90-day growth sprints. Build me a complete 90-day plan. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS] Current monthly revenue: [APPROXIMATE NUMBER OR RANGE] My #1 goal for the next 90 days: [BE SPECIFIC — E.G., "GO FROM $8K TO $15K MONTHLY REVENUE" OR "LAUNCH MY ONLINE COURSE"] Biggest bottleneck right now: [WHAT IS BLOCKING GROWTH] Hours per week I can dedicate to growth work (not day-to-day operations): [NUMBER] Resources available: [BUDGET FOR ADS/TOOLS, TEAM MEMBERS, ETC.] Create a 90-day sprint plan with: 1. THREE 30-day phases, each with a clear theme and milestone: - Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Foundation — what systems or assets need to be built first? - Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Execution — what gets launched, tested, or scaled? - Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Optimization — what gets refined based on data? 2. For each phase, provide: - The specific milestone that marks phase completion - Weekly actions broken into tasks (max 5 tasks per week, respecting the hours I stated) - One key metric to track that week - Potential blockers and how to handle them 3. A "kill criteria" list: what signals should make me pivot or abandon this plan early rather than wasting 90 days on the wrong strategy. Be realistic about what one person (or a small team) can accomplish. Do not assume unlimited time or budget. Every task should be concrete enough that I know exactly what to do when I sit down to work.

The "kill criteria" is the secret weapon here. Most planning prompts generate optimistic roadmaps. By asking ChatGPT to define failure signals upfront, you build in the strategic awareness to pivot early. The hour-per-week constraint forces realistic task sizing — fill that in honestly.

6 Pricing Strategy

You are a pricing strategy consultant who works with small businesses and entrepreneurs. Help me develop a pricing strategy for my product/service. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS] What I sell: [PRODUCT OR SERVICE, DESCRIBE IN DETAIL] Current price: [WHAT YOU CHARGE NOW, OR "NOT YET PRICED"] My costs per unit/engagement: [DIRECT COSTS — MATERIALS, TIME, SOFTWARE, SUBCONTRACTORS] My monthly fixed costs: [RENT, SUBSCRIPTIONS, INSURANCE, ETC.] Competitor pricing: [WHAT SIMILAR BUSINESSES CHARGE, IF KNOWN] My target customer: [WHO THEY ARE AND THEIR BUDGET SENSITIVITY] Provide: 1. Cost Analysis: Calculate my true cost per unit/engagement including allocated overhead. What is my minimum viable price to break even? 2. Competitor Benchmark: Based on the competitor info I provided, where does my current pricing sit? Am I underpricing (common for small businesses) or overpricing? 3. Three Pricing Models to consider, with pros/cons for each: - Model A: Cost-plus pricing (margin-based) - Model B: Value-based pricing (based on outcome delivered to customer) - Model C: Tiered/package pricing (good-better-best structure) 4. For Model C, design three specific tiers with names, prices, and what is included in each. Use anchoring psychology: the middle tier should be the obvious choice. 5. Price Presentation: How should I frame the price on my website or in proposals? Include specific language and framing techniques (e.g., per-day breakdown, comparison anchors, ROI framing). 6. A recommended starting price with reasoning, and a plan for testing price sensitivity over the next 60 days. Be specific with numbers, not ranges. Give me a concrete recommendation, not "it depends."

The instruction "be specific with numbers, not ranges" and "give me a concrete recommendation" forces ChatGPT past its tendency to hedge. The three-model comparison gives you genuine strategic options, and the tiered pricing design is something most small business owners never build but always benefit from.

Operations Prompts

7 Standard Operating Procedure Writer

You are an operations consultant who writes SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) for small businesses. Write a complete SOP for the following process. The process: [DESCRIBE THE PROCESS — E.G., "ONBOARDING A NEW CLIENT FROM SIGNED CONTRACT TO KICKOFF MEETING" OR "PROCESSING AND SHIPPING AN ORDER"] Who performs this process: [ROLE — E.G., "ME," "MY ASSISTANT," "ANY TEAM MEMBER"] Tools/software involved: [LIST ALL TOOLS USED — E.G., GOOGLE SHEETS, STRIPE, MAILCHIMP, SLACK] Current pain points: [WHAT GOES WRONG OR GETS MISSED IN THIS PROCESS] Frequency: [HOW OFTEN THIS PROCESS HAPPENS — DAILY, WEEKLY, PER CLIENT, ETC.] Write the SOP with: 1. Purpose: One sentence on why this process exists and what successful completion looks like. 2. Scope: Who does this, when it is triggered, and when it is considered complete. 3. Prerequisites: What must be true before starting (e.g., contract signed, payment received). 4. Step-by-Step Procedure: Numbered steps. Each step should include: - The specific action (detailed enough that someone doing it for the first time could follow) - Which tool to use and where to click/navigate - What the expected output or result of that step is - Common mistakes to avoid at that step 5. Escalation: What to do if something goes wrong or is outside normal parameters. 6. Quality Check: A 5-point checklist to verify the process was completed correctly. 7. Time Estimate: How long this process should take. Write for someone with no prior knowledge of this process. Avoid assumptions. If a step involves copying information from one place to another, specify exactly which fields.

The "common mistakes to avoid at that step" instruction is what separates this from a basic checklist. It captures institutional knowledge that usually only lives in your head. If you are ever planning to hire or delegate, SOPs written at this level of detail are the single most valuable asset you can build.

8 Client Onboarding Sequence

You are a client experience consultant for service-based small businesses. Design a complete client onboarding sequence for my business. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR SERVICE BUSINESS] What the client just purchased: [THE SERVICE OR PACKAGE] Typical project timeline: [HOW LONG DOES THE ENGAGEMENT LAST] What I need from the client to start work: [INFORMATION, ASSETS, ACCESS, DECISIONS] Common client frustrations during onboarding: [WHAT CAUSES CONFUSION OR DELAYS] Design a complete onboarding sequence including: 1. Welcome Email (send immediately after purchase): - Full email copy, warm but professional - What happens next (set clear expectations with dates) - What you need from them (specific list with deadlines) - Link to intake form/questionnaire 2. Intake Questionnaire: - 10-15 questions that capture everything you need to begin - Organized by category (business info, project specifics, preferences, logistics) - Include "why we ask this" notes for questions that might seem unusual 3. Kickoff Meeting Agenda: - Suggested duration - Topics to cover with time allocations - Questions to ask the client - Decisions that need to be made in this meeting 4. Post-Kickoff Follow-Up Email: - Summary of decisions made - Timeline with specific milestones - Communication expectations (how and how often you will update them) - What they can expect to see/receive first, and when 5. Onboarding Checklist: - Internal checklist for you/your team to ensure nothing falls through the cracks - Items grouped by: Before Kickoff, During Kickoff, Within 48 Hours After Kickoff Every touchpoint should reduce client anxiety and make them feel confident they made the right choice.

The line "every touchpoint should reduce client anxiety" reframes onboarding from an administrative task to a trust-building exercise. The intake questionnaire with "why we ask this" notes is a subtle professional touch that most businesses skip. Customize the common frustrations field from your real experience.

9 Hiring Job Description

You are an HR consultant who helps small businesses write job postings that attract great candidates and filter out poor fits. Write a complete job description for the following role. Role title: [JOB TITLE] My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS, SIZE, AND CULTURE] What this person will do day-to-day: [LIST 3-5 CORE RESPONSIBILITIES] Must-have skills: [NON-NEGOTIABLE REQUIREMENTS] Nice-to-have skills: [PREFERRED BUT NOT REQUIRED] Compensation range: [SALARY/HOURLY RANGE, OR "COMPETITIVE - HELP ME DETERMINE"] Work arrangement: [REMOTE, HYBRID, IN-OFFICE, LOCATION] Write the job description with these sections: 1. Opening Hook (3-4 sentences): Sell the opportunity. What makes this role exciting? What will this person build or own? Avoid generic "fast-paced environment" language. 2. About the Company (3-4 sentences): What we do, who we serve, and what it is like to work here. Be honest and specific, not aspirational. 3. What You Will Do: 5-7 bullet points. Each starts with an action verb and describes a concrete outcome, not a vague responsibility. Include the percentage of time spent on each if possible. 4. What You Bring: Split into "Required" (hard requirements only, maximum 5) and "Bonus" (nice-to-haves, maximum 3). Be ruthlessly honest about which is which — listing 15 "requirements" drives away strong candidates. 5. Red Flags (internal, not published): 5 interview questions or screening criteria that help me identify candidates who look good on paper but would be a poor fit for this role specifically. For each, explain what a concerning answer would sound like. 6. What We Offer: Compensation, benefits, and any unique perks. If I did not provide compensation, suggest a market-rate range based on the role and responsibilities. Write the public-facing sections (1-4, 6) in a tone that is professional but human. Avoid corporate jargon. A strong candidate should read this and think "that sounds like a real place run by real people."

The Red Flags section is for your eyes only, but it is the most valuable part. Most small business owners hire on vibes and regret it. Having pre-defined screening criteria tied to the specific role keeps your interviews focused. The "maximum 5 requirements" constraint also fights the common tendency to list a wish list that scares off qualified applicants.

Content & SEO Prompts

10 SEO Blog Post Outline + Draft

You are an SEO content strategist who writes blog posts that rank on Google and actually help readers. Write a complete blog post for my business. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS] Target keyword: [THE MAIN KEYWORD I WANT TO RANK FOR] Secondary keywords: [2-4 RELATED KEYWORDS TO INCLUDE NATURALLY] Search intent: [WHAT IS THE READER TRYING TO ACCOMPLISH WHEN THEY SEARCH THIS?] My audience: [WHO READS MY BLOG AND WHAT LEVEL OF EXPERTISE DO THEY HAVE?] Word count target: [E.G., 1500-2000 WORDS] Create: 1. SEO-Optimized Title: 3 options. Each under 60 characters, includes the target keyword, and has a compelling hook (number, question, or power word). 2. Meta Description: 155 characters max, includes target keyword, has a clear value proposition and implied CTA. 3. Detailed Outline: - H1 (title) - H2 sections (5-8) with H3 subsections where appropriate - For each section, note: key point to make, secondary keyword to include, approximate word count - Include an FAQ section (4-5 questions) structured for featured snippet capture 4. Full Draft: - Write the complete post following the outline - Opening paragraph must hook the reader and include the target keyword in the first 100 words - Use short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max) - Include specific examples, numbers, or frameworks — no filler - Natural keyword placement (target keyword 4-6 times, secondary keywords 1-2 times each) - End with a clear CTA relevant to my business - Write in a tone that matches my brand, not generic "blog voice" 5. Internal Linking Suggestions: 3-5 topics for related blog posts I should write that this post could link to (building a content cluster). Do not keyword-stuff. Every mention of a keyword should feel natural in the sentence. Prioritize being genuinely helpful over being optimized.

The "prioritize being genuinely helpful over being optimized" instruction prevents the robotic SEO content that readers (and increasingly, Google) reject. The internal linking suggestions turn a single post into the start of a content strategy. Fill in the search intent field carefully — it changes the entire angle of the article.

11 Customer Case Study

You are a content writer who creates customer case studies that build trust and drive sales for small businesses. Write a case study based on the information I provide. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS] Client name/company: [NAME, OR "ANONYMOUS" IF NEEDED] Client's industry: [WHAT THEY DO] The problem they had before working with me: [SPECIFIC CHALLENGE — BE DETAILED] What I/we did for them: [THE SERVICE OR SOLUTION PROVIDED — INCLUDE TIMELINE] The results: [SPECIFIC OUTCOMES — NUMBERS, PERCENTAGES, TIMEFRAMES, QUALITATIVE CHANGES] A quote from the client (if available): [DIRECT QUOTE, OR "PLEASE DRAFT A REALISTIC QUOTE BASED ON THE RESULTS"] Write the case study using this framework: 1. Headline: "[Client Name] + [Specific Result]" format. E.g., "How River Coffee Increased Online Orders by 340% in 90 Days" 2. Snapshot Box: A quick-reference box with: - Client: [name/industry] - Challenge: [one sentence] - Solution: [one sentence] - Result: [key metric] - Timeline: [how long] 3. The Challenge (200-300 words): Paint a vivid picture of their situation before. What was at stake? What had they already tried? Make the reader see themselves in this client. 4. The Solution (200-300 words): What specifically did you do? Walk through the approach without giving away your entire methodology. Focus on the strategic thinking, not just the deliverables. 5. The Results (200-300 words): Lead with the biggest, most specific number. Then layer in secondary results. Include both quantitative metrics and qualitative changes (e.g., "the owner was able to take weekends off for the first time in two years"). 6. Key Takeaway: One paragraph — what should a prospective client learn from this story? End with an implied CTA. Write in third person. Tone should be confident and factual, not salesy. Let the results do the selling.

The Snapshot Box is critical because many readers will skim before deciding to read the full case study. The instruction to "let the results do the selling" prevents the puffery that makes most case studies feel like ads. If you do not have exact numbers, give ChatGPT your best estimates and it will frame them properly.

12 Newsletter Content Calendar

You are a content strategist who helps small business owners plan consistent, valuable email newsletters. Create a 12-week (quarterly) newsletter content calendar for my business. My business: [DESCRIBE YOUR BUSINESS] Newsletter frequency: [WEEKLY, BIWEEKLY, MONTHLY] My audience: [WHO SUBSCRIBES AND WHY THEY SIGNED UP] Business goals for this quarter: [WHAT DO I WANT TO ACHIEVE?] Topics I can write about: [YOUR AREAS OF EXPERTISE, COMMON CUSTOMER QUESTIONS] Products/services to promote this quarter: [WHAT ARE YOU SELLING?] For each newsletter issue, provide: - Issue number and send date (starting next week) - Theme/topic - Subject line (2 options: one curiosity-driven, one benefit-driven) - Content structure: * Opening hook (2-3 sentences to draft) * Main content angle (what specific insight, story, or advice to share) * Secondary content block (a quick tip, resource, or behind-the-scenes note) * CTA (what action do you want readers to take — not always a sale) - Content type tag: EDUCATE / INSPIRE / ENTERTAIN / SELL Follow this quarterly balance: - 50% EDUCATE (teach something useful, position yourself as expert) - 20% INSPIRE (customer stories, personal journey, industry trends) - 15% ENTERTAIN (behind-the-scenes, hot takes, personality-driven) - 15% SELL (direct promotion of product/service, always paired with value) Never schedule two SELL issues back-to-back. Each issue should be self-contained and valuable even if the reader never buys anything. Present as a table: Issue # | Date | Theme | Subject Lines | Type | Main Angle | CTA.

The percentage-based content balance solves the two newsletter failure modes: all selling (people unsubscribe) or all value with no selling (no revenue). The "valuable even if the reader never buys anything" constraint ensures every issue earns the right to stay in someone's inbox. Adjust the frequency to what you will actually stick to.

Pro Tips: How to Get Better Results

  • Be specific about your business. "I run a business" produces generic output. "I run a 3-person residential painting company in Calgary that does $40K/month, mostly from referrals, and I want to break into commercial contracts" produces output you can actually use. Every bracketed field in these prompts is an opportunity to give ChatGPT context. Fill them all in.
  • Always include context about your audience. Who your customer is changes everything about the output. A pricing strategy for luxury wedding photography is completely different from one for budget web design. Tell ChatGPT who you serve and what they care about.
  • Iterate on outputs. Your first result is a draft, not a deliverable. Follow up with "Make the tone more conversational," "Add more specific examples," or "This section is too generic — rewrite it with a concrete scenario about [X]." The best results come from a conversation, not a single prompt.
  • Use follow-up prompts to go deeper. After getting a 90-day plan, ask "Now break down Week 1 into daily tasks." After a pricing analysis, ask "Now write the pricing page copy using Tier 2 as the anchor." Chaining prompts is where ChatGPT becomes a genuine business tool and not just a novelty.
  • Save what works. When a prompt produces great output for your business, save it with your custom context already filled in. You have just built a reusable tool tailored to your specific situation. That library compounds in value over time.
